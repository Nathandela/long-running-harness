# Privacy Engineering and Differential Privacy: Formal Guarantees, Anonymity Models, Synthetic Data, and GDPR-Oriented Engineering

*[28 February 2026]*

## Abstract

This survey examines privacy engineering techniques that provide formal or semi-formal guarantees for data processing, with particular attention to differential privacy, k-anonymity-family models, synthetic data generation, and GDPR-oriented technical compliance frameworks. It documents how these approaches formalize privacy risks as indistinguishability, reidentification, or inference bounds, and how they compare to softer engineering practices such as pseudonymisation and access control under contemporary regulatory guidance. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/1906.01337)

The analysis organizes the landscape into eight approach families: syntactic anonymity models (k-anonymity, l-diversity, t-closeness), central and local differential privacy, differentially private synthetic data, non-DP synthetic data, cryptographic privacy-preserving computation, query-oriented statistical disclosure control, and formalized privacy engineering for GDPR, including pseudonymisation and risk-based frameworks such as the NIST Privacy Framework. This structure reveals trade-offs among utility losses, composable guarantees, adversary models, regulatory interpretability, and implementation complexity, without prescribing a preferred choice for specific deployment contexts. [petsymposium](https://petsymposium.org/popets/2024/popets-2024-0013.pdf)

## 1. Introduction

The problem addressed by this survey concerns how modern privacy engineering captures and mitigates risks of reidentification and attribute inference in data analysis workflows using formal or quasi-formal guarantees, and how these guarantees relate to regulatory constructs such as anonymisation, pseudonymisation, and data protection by design under the GDPR. This question matters because organizations now routinely process high-dimensional personal data at scale, and naive de-identification has repeatedly failed in the face of linkage and inference attacks, leading to both privacy harms and regulatory uncertainty. [paper.ijcsns](http://paper.ijcsns.org/07_book/201712/20171225.pdf)

The scope of the survey includes: syntactic anonymity models (k-anonymity, l-diversity, t-closeness), central and local differential privacy and their variants, synthetic data generation with and without differential privacy, cryptographic privacy-preserving computation, query-oriented disclosure control, and engineering frameworks for GDPR compliance such as pseudonymisation techniques and the NIST Privacy Framework. This survey explicitly excludes general organizational governance, contract law, and non-technical compliance topics except where they are tightly coupled to technical design choices, and it does not attempt an exhaustive treatment of all cryptographic primitives beyond their role as privacy-enhancing technologies in deployed systems. [enisa.europa](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/publications/Data%20Sharing%20Report.pdf)

Three key definitions underpin the discussion. First, “formal privacy guarantee” refers to a mathematically specified property of a mechanism (for example, \(\epsilon\)-differential privacy or k-anonymity) that bounds an attacker’s ability to distinguish neighboring datasets or records given the output. Second, “syntactic anonymity” denotes models such as k-anonymity, l-diversity, and t-closeness that operate on released tables by constraining equivalence classes over quasi-identifiers and sensitive attributes, rather than perturbing query outputs. Third, “synthetic data” is used for artificially generated datasets that approximate the joint distribution of an original dataset, either with formal guarantees (for example, DP synthetic data) or with heuristic protections subject to known attacks such as membership inference and membership collision. [unece](https://unece.org/fileadmin/DAM/stats/documents/ece/ces/ge.46/2011/26_Dwork-Smith.pdf)

## 2. Foundations

The foundational concept for many approaches is indistinguishability: a privacy-preserving mechanism attempts to ensure that an attacker observing its output cannot confidently distinguish between neighboring inputs (for example, datasets differing in a single individual) beyond specified parameters. In differential privacy, this principle is formalized by requiring that the probability of any output under two neighboring datasets differs by at most a multiplicative factor \(\exp(\epsilon)\) and, in the approximate variant, an additive \(\delta\), which yields strong resilience to arbitrary side information and post-processing. Syntactic anonymity models, by contrast, enforce indistinguishability at the level of equivalence classes in released microdata, requiring each record to share quasi-identifiers with at least \(k-1\) others and possibly constraining sensitive attribute distributions. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2201.00099.pdf)

Central differential privacy (sometimes called the curator model) assumes a trusted data curator who has access to raw data and answers queries by adding calibrated random noise, typically proportional to a global sensitivity bound divided by \(\epsilon\). Local differential privacy moves randomization to the data subjects or client devices themselves, requiring each user to perturb their own data before transmission, thus removing the need to trust a central curator but increasing variance and often the sample complexity of statistical estimation. Synthetic data techniques can be viewed as learning an approximate generative model of the original data and then releasing samples from it; when the learning and sampling algorithms satisfy differential privacy, the resulting synthetic dataset inherits the formal DP guarantees, whereas non-DP generative models expose training populations to attacks such as membership inference, membership collision, and reconstruction. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.04710)

Regulatory frameworks add a second conceptual layer. Under the GDPR, anonymised data lies outside the regulation when reidentification is no longer “reasonably likely,” whereas pseudonymised data remains personal data but benefits from being treated as a safeguard under Articles 4(5), 25, and 32. Opinion 05/2014 of the Article 29 Working Party and subsequent ENISA reports emphasize that many technical anonymisation techniques (for example, k-anonymity and basic masking) often yield only pseudonymisation in practice, and that robust anonymisation must consider linkage attacks, background knowledge, and specific contexts of use. The NIST Privacy Framework and related guidance treat formal privacy technologies as one class of controls within a broader risk management approach that also accounts for inference risks at system level, such as reidentification and operational attribute inference. [pwc](https://www.pwc.lu/en/general-data-protection/docs/pwc-anonymisation-and-pseudonymisation.pdf)

## 4. Analysis

### 4.1 Syntactic anonymity models: k-anonymity, l-diversity, t-closeness (Approach A)

**Theory & mechanism.** Syntactic anonymity models operate on microdata tables by transforming quasi-identifiers (for example, age, ZIP code) through generalization and suppression so that each resulting equivalence class contains at least \(k\) records, thereby making any individual record indistinguishable among those \(k\) records with respect to the quasi-identifiers. The l-diversity extension further constrains sensitive attributes within each equivalence class to contain at least \(l\) “well represented” distinct values, which aims to reduce homogeneity and background-knowledge attacks. T-closeness adds a requirement that the distance between the sensitive attribute distribution in any equivalence class and the overall table distribution does not exceed a threshold \(t\), often measured using Earth Mover’s Distance, thereby bounding the information gain about sensitive attributes. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1301.1751.pdf)

**Literature evidence.** Samarati and Sweeney introduced k-anonymity in the late 1990s as a formalization of quasi-identifier based anonymisation, leveraging generalization and suppression to allow controlled disclosure while bounding identity disclosure risk. Machanavajjhala et al. subsequently showed that k-anonymity remains vulnerable to homogeneity and background-knowledge attacks, proposing l-diversity as a stronger notion ensuring sufficient diversity of sensitive attributes in equivalence classes. Li et al. then proposed t-closeness to address skewness and similarity attacks by requiring sensitive attribute distributions in classes to be close to the global distribution, and they also analyzed its relationship to differential privacy. Later work surveys and compares these techniques, including their application to medical data and social network graphs, documenting both their utility advantages and susceptibility to high-dimensional reidentification. [semanticscholar](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/6f85830bf7c8418f02d8bdca367ed64450e92b4d)

**Implementations & benchmarks.** Numerous anonymisation algorithms implement k-anonymity, l-diversity, and t-closeness through clustering or optimization, including greedy clustering and information-theoretic methods that attempt to minimize information loss while satisfying given thresholds for \(k\) and \(t\). Empirical studies report that satisfying stricter t-closeness constraints often requires large equivalence classes, which increases generalization and leads to measurable degradation in downstream machine learning performance compared with less strict k-anonymity configurations. Network-specific variants, such as k-degree anonymity and extensions enforcing l-diversity and t-closeness on graph properties, show that structural anonymisation can preserve some graph statistics while substantially altering local structure, with information loss ratios and utility metrics reported across social network datasets. [rroij](https://www.rroij.com/open-access/proposing-a-novel-synergized-kdegree-ldiversity-tclosenessmodel-for-graph-based-data-anonymization.pdf)

**Strengths & limitations.** Syntactic anonymity models align closely with traditional data publishing workflows and yield interpretable protections (for example, each individual appears among at least \(k\) records) while preserving exact data for non-quasi-identifier attributes, which supports some forms of analysis without added noise. However, they can be vulnerable to linkage attacks on high-dimensional data, they lack robust composition properties once multiple releases or external data are considered, and they provide no intrinsic protection against attribute inference beyond what specific parameterizations and data distributions afford. Computational complexity is another limitation: t-closeness optimization under realistic models is NP-hard, leading to heuristic implementations whose privacy and utility trade-offs can vary widely across datasets and parameter choices. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2102.04763.pdf)

### 4.2 Central differential privacy (Approach B)

**Theory & mechanism.** Central differential privacy defines a randomized mechanism \(M\) as \((\epsilon,\delta)\)-differentially private if, for all neighboring datasets differing in one individual and all measurable output sets, the probability of outputs differs by at most a multiplicative factor \(\exp(\epsilon)\) plus additive \(\delta\). This definition yields strong semantic guarantees: the presence or absence of any individual has limited influence on the distribution of outputs, and the guarantee composes over multiple analyses, with basic composition implying that running \(k\) mechanisms each with parameters \((\epsilon,\delta)\) yields \((k\epsilon,k\delta)\)-DP, while advanced composition yields tighter bounds. Mechanisms such as the Laplace, Gaussian, and randomized response mechanisms achieve DP by adding noise calibrated to the global sensitivity of the query function, with post-processing immunity ensuring that subsequent transformations do not weaken the guarantee. [labelia](https://www.labelia.org/en/blog/the-nuts-and-bolts-of-differential-privacy-part1)

**Literature evidence.** Dwork and colleagues introduced differential privacy around 2006 and subsequently developed its algorithmic foundations, documenting privacy properties such as group privacy, robustness to side information, and composability. A rich literature now characterizes central DP as a de facto standard for privacy-preserving data analysis, with surveys such as “SoK: Differential Privacies” classifying numerous variants and extensions (for example, concentrated DP, zero-concentrated DP, Rényi DP) and their relationships. Overviews aimed at non-technical audiences and policymakers emphasize how DP addresses failures of traditional de-identification and describe its deployment in official statistics and technology companies, highlighting inherent privacy-utility trade-offs. [dash.harvard](https://dash.harvard.edu/bitstream/1/38323292/1/4_Wood_Final.pdf)

**Implementations & benchmarks.** Practical implementations include differentially private histograms, streaming algorithms under continual observation, and DP mechanisms for complex query workloads, with formal error bounds and empirical evaluations across synthetic and real datasets. Large-scale deployments, such as the U.S. Census Bureau’s adoption of DP for 2020 census tabulations, provide case studies of how privacy budgets, accuracy targets, and fairness concerns are negotiated, although many details appear in institutional reports rather than academic benchmarks. Open-source libraries and frameworks (for example, implementations described in DP tutorials and primers) measure trade-offs between privacy parameters and query accuracy, often documenting that small \(\epsilon\) values can dramatically increase error for sparse or fine-grained statistics. [arxiv](http://arxiv.org/pdf/2306.10428.pdf)

**Strengths & limitations.** Central DP provides strong, mathematically composable guarantees that are largely agnostic to the attacker’s auxiliary information and robust under post-processing, which distinguishes it from syntactic anonymity models. At the same time, it assumes a trusted curator, and many mechanisms exhibit significant accuracy degradation for small \(\epsilon\), especially for complex queries or small populations, which constrains practical deployments. Selecting and communicating an appropriate privacy budget remains contentious: empirical work on policy applications indicates that stakeholders struggle to interpret \(\epsilon\) in terms of concrete risks, and miscalibration can undercut either privacy or utility expectations. [arxiv](http://arxiv.org/pdf/2409.11680.pdf)

### 4.3 Local differential privacy (Approach C)

**Theory & mechanism.** Local differential privacy (LDP) shifts the DP requirement from a trusted curator to the individual data contributors by requiring that each client-side mechanism mapping a user’s true value to a reported value satisfies \(\epsilon\)-DP at the user level. Classic mechanisms include randomized response and its variants, where each user perturbs their answer independently according to specified probabilities, as well as more sophisticated schemes like RAPPOR that encode values into Bloom filters and apply permanent and instantaneous randomized response steps. The aggregator then estimates aggregate statistics, such as frequency distributions or means, from the noisy reports, with accuracy depending on the number of users, the dimensionality of the domain, and the chosen mechanism parameters. [research.google](https://research.google.com/pubs/archive/42852.pdf)

**Literature evidence.** Surveys on LDP document its emergence as a powerful model for privacy-preserving analytics without a trusted curator, summarizing privacy models, mechanisms, and applications across domains including telemetry, location data, and recommendation systems. Cormode et al. present a tutorial on privacy at scale with LDP, explaining how major technology companies have deployed LDP-based telemetry systems and detailing open problems around communication efficiency and utility. Erlingsson et al.’s RAPPOR paper formalizes a locally differentially private mechanism for collecting categorical statistics at scale and proves DP properties of its two-stage randomized response design, while subsequent analyses refine its utility and privacy properties. [emergentmind](https://www.emergentmind.com/topics/rappor)

**Implementations & benchmarks.** Implementations of LDP mechanisms have been evaluated for tasks such as discrete distribution estimation, hierarchical aggregation of numerical data, and utility-optimized reporting when some attributes are non-sensitive, often using large-scale real datasets to benchmark mean squared error and divergence metrics. Results typically show that LDP mechanisms require substantially larger populations or higher \(\epsilon\) values than central DP to achieve comparable accuracy, reflecting the higher variance introduced by client-side perturbation, especially in high-dimensional or sparse settings. Recent work also investigates LDP’s interactions with fairness, showing that accuracy degradation can be uneven across subgroups and highlighting a need for formal analyses of subgroup-specific error under local privacy constraints. [arxiv](http://arxiv.org/pdf/1807.11317.pdf)

**Strengths & limitations.** LDP eliminates the need to trust a central curator with raw data, which aligns with scenarios where central storage of sensitive attributes is unacceptable or heavily regulated, and it can be easier to justify under data minimization principles. However, LDP’s statistical efficiency is generally worse than central DP, often requiring quadratically more reports for comparable accuracy, and designing mechanisms for multidimensional or continuous data remains challenging, particularly when different attributes warrant different privacy levels. Practical deployments must also manage issues such as longitudinal privacy, communication overhead, and client implementation correctness, since any deviation from the specified perturbation can undermine formal guarantees. [sciencedirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0920548923001083)

### 4.4 Differentially private synthetic data (Approach D)

**Theory & mechanism.** Differentially private synthetic data generation treats DP as a constraint on a learning and sampling pipeline: a DP mechanism first learns a model of the original data (for example, via DP histograms, DP Bayesian networks, or DP deep generative models), then releases samples from the model as synthetic data. Because the learning procedure itself satisfies DP with respect to the original dataset, any post-processed synthetic dataset inherits the formal privacy guarantee, with the privacy budget \(\epsilon\) governing how closely the synthetic distribution can approximate the true data. Evaluation typically distinguishes between utility (for example, predictive performance, preservation of marginal distributions, clustering structure) and privacy (for example, resistance to membership inference, attribute inference, and reconstruction attacks), under the chosen \((\epsilon,\delta)\). [ece.nus.edu](https://www.ece.nus.edu.sg/stfpage/bsikdar/papers/tcss_razi_25.pdf)

**Literature evidence.** Recent applied work demonstrates DP synthetic data in sensitive domains such as behavioral health, where mechanisms like the Adaptive Iterative Mechanism (AIM) generate DP synthetic versions of wearable and survey data and evaluate them across a range of privacy budgets using predictive and distributional metrics. The study reports that mid-range \(\epsilon\) values (for example, 5–10) preserved key statistical relationships and predictive power while significantly mitigating linkage attacks that succeeded on de-identified non-DP releases, documenting a concrete privacy-utility trade-off. Broader analyses compare DP synthetic data, anonymised data, and non-DP synthetic data across tasks and datasets, finding that synthetic data can maintain higher predictive accuracy than some anonymised or heavily perturbed DP releases, while DP constraints still introduce distortions that must be quantified. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.02971v1)

**Implementations & benchmarks.** Implementations range from DP histogram-based synthesizers to DP generative adversarial networks and variational autoencoders, often evaluated in competitions and benchmarks such as the NeurIPS synthetic data competition, which concluded that adding noise directly sometimes outperformed complex generative models under certain privacy-utility metrics. Benchmarking frameworks measure utility using classification accuracy, regression error, correlation preservation, and unsupervised structure (for example, via UMAP projections), while privacy is probed through membership inference and membership collision attacks under varying budgets. Studies document that tighter privacy budgets increase distortion and reduce attack success rates, whereas looser budgets approach non-private performance but weaken formal protections, emphasizing the importance of context-specific evaluation. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.03668v1)

**Strengths & limitations.** DP synthetic data enables sharing of datasets with formal privacy guarantees while allowing recipients to perform arbitrary analyses without direct interaction with a curator, which is attractive for research and open data contexts. However, achieving high utility for complex, high-dimensional data is challenging, and DP constraints can disproportionately harm underrepresented subgroups whose patterns are difficult to learn under noise, as documented in attacks focusing on low-density regions. Moreover, the guarantees apply to the original dataset, not to individuals outside the sampling frame, and interpreting DP synthetic data under legal frameworks that hinge on “reasonably likely” reidentification remains an area of ongoing discussion rather than settled practice. [proceedings.mlr](https://proceedings.mlr.press/v206/breugel23a/breugel23a.pdf)

### 4.5 Non-DP synthetic data with heuristic privacy (Approach E)

**Theory & mechanism.** Non-DP synthetic data approaches use generative models such as GANs or VAEs trained on real datasets without differential privacy constraints, possibly combined with ad hoc privacy controls such as clipping outliers, restricting sample diversity, or applying post-hoc filters, to produce synthetic tables or images intended to mask individuals while preserving utility. Privacy in this setting is typically conceptualized in terms of specific attack models (for example, membership inference, attribute inference, reconstruction, or membership collision) rather than a global indistinguishability bound, and metrics focus on attack success rates, distances between distributions, or overfitting indicators. This framework treats privacy as an empirical property of a model under chosen tests, which can be tuned or recalibrated as new attacks emerge. [staff.fnwi.uva](https://staff.fnwi.uva.nl/a.s.z.belloum/LiteratureStudies/Reports/2022_ChenghanSong.pdf)

**Literature evidence.** Surveys on privacy-preserving generative models document a variety of attacks against GAN-based and VAE-based synthetic data, classifying them by whether they target membership, attributes, or model parameters, and summarizing metrics such as accuracy, precision, and reconstruction error used to evaluate attack success. Van Breugel et al. introduce DOMIAS, a membership inference attack that uses density estimation to detect overfitting in generative models, and show that it significantly improves attack performance, especially for underrepresented groups and rare samples in tabular data. The NeurIPS synthetic data competition and related studies report that, in several cases, simple additive-noise baselines achieved better privacy-utility trade-offs than complex non-DP generative models, highlighting the difficulty of ensuring privacy empirically without formal guarantees. [ece.nus.edu](https://www.ece.nus.edu.sg/stfpage/bsikdar/papers/tcss_razi_25.pdf)

**Implementations & benchmarks.** Implemented systems include generic tabular data synthesizers, domain-specific GANs for healthcare, and commercial tools that generate synthetic customer or transaction data, often advertising privacy benefits while relying on proprietary evaluation methodologies. Academic benchmarks commonly compare multiple synthesizers across datasets and adversarial scenarios, using membership inference attacks (including shadow-model and gradient-based methods) and membership collision attacks that detect synthetic samples close to training records, with results showing substantial variation in vulnerability across models and datasets. These evaluations also emphasize that overfitting and rarity of samples act as key drivers of privacy risk: synthetic data tends to leak more information about rare or underrepresented groups, complicating fairness considerations. [jparras.github](https://jparras.github.io/files/2025-mia-ojcs.pdf)

**Strengths & limitations.** Non-DP synthetic data can achieve high utility and realistic distributions, sometimes surpassing DP mechanisms in predictive accuracy and structural fidelity, particularly when there are no formal constraints on noise injection or sensitivity. Nevertheless, the absence of a global, composable guarantee leaves privacy protection dependent on the set of tested attacks, and new or more powerful attacks can retrospectively invalidate previous assurances, especially for small or high-dimensional datasets. Moreover, mapping empirical attack success or failure to legal standards such as “reasonable likelihood” of reidentification under GDPR remains conceptually and jurisprudentially unsettled, which can complicate claims that synthetic data has achieved anonymisation rather than pseudonymisation. [edpb.europa](https://www.edpb.europa.eu/system/files/2025-01/edpb_guidelines_202501_pseudonymisation_en.pdf)

### 4.6 Cryptographic privacy-preserving computation (Approach F)

**Theory & mechanism.** Cryptographic privacy-preserving computation encompasses techniques that maintain data in encrypted or otherwise protected form while enabling computation, such as secure multiparty computation (MPC), homomorphic encryption, proxy re-encryption, and attribute-based encryption. These techniques provide confidentiality and access control guarantees based on hardness assumptions, ensuring that parties learn at most the agreed outputs and no additional information about inputs, absent cryptographic breaks or side-channel leaks. Proxy re-encryption and attribute-based encryption additionally support fine-grained, cryptographically enforced access policies, enabling user-controlled data sharing in untrusted infrastructures such as clouds, while limiting who can decrypt which data objects. [ismsforum](https://www.ismsforum.es/ficheros/descargas/enisa-report---data-protection.pdf)

**Literature evidence.** ENISA’s report on engineering personal data sharing discusses advanced cryptographic solutions, including attribute-based encryption and proxy re-encryption, as key building blocks for privacy-preserving architectures that support data protection by design and by default under GDPR. A companion ENISA report on data pseudonymisation and advanced techniques further examines cryptographic pseudonymisation schemes and their integration into risk-based security and privacy engineering processes, emphasizing that cryptographic mechanisms must be combined with organizational controls to achieve effective safeguards. Broader privacy engineering analyses, such as those applying the NIST Privacy Framework in case studies, classify cryptographic techniques as privacy-enhancing technologies that mitigate reidentification and unauthorized access risks when appropriately integrated into system design. [enisa.europa](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/publications/ENISA%20Report%20-%20Data%20Pseudonymisation%20-%20Advanced%20Techniques%20and%20Use%20Cases.pdf)

**Implementations & benchmarks.** Practical implementations include cloud storage systems where data are encrypted under attribute-based policies, with performance benchmarks reporting overheads in key management and decryption, and proxy re-encryption based architectures where users encrypt data with their own keys and delegate selective access to third parties via re-encryption tokens. These systems are evaluated using metrics such as encryption and re-encryption throughput, latency, scalability of key distribution, and robustness against threat models involving untrusted clouds or processors, with case studies in sectors like healthcare and identity management. While many cryptographic PETs are prototyped in research or niche products, commercial adoption tends to be selective and often focused on high-value or high-risk use cases, reflecting trade-offs between performance and protection. [enisa.europa](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/publications/Data%20Sharing%20Report.pdf)

**Strengths & limitations.** Cryptographic computation techniques can prevent raw data exposure even to infrastructure operators, reducing many confidentiality risks and supporting strong access control semantics compatible with legal requirements for security of processing. However, they do not in themselves bound what can be inferred from the agreed outputs, so they complement rather than replace indistinguishability-based privacy notions such as DP or k-anonymity, especially when outputs are statistical aggregates or models. Furthermore, performance overheads, key management complexity, and integration challenges can be substantial, and evidence on large-scale, long-term deployments remains less developed than for other privacy engineering approaches, leaving some questions about operational resilience and usability. [enisa.europa](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/publications/pseudonymisation-techniques-and-best-practices)

### 4.7 Query-oriented statistical disclosure control and access control (Approach G)

**Theory & mechanism.** Query-oriented statistical disclosure control (SDC) and safe settings combine access control, query vetting, and output perturbation or suppression to manage disclosure risks in interactive data access environments, typically through rules such as minimum cell counts, noise injection, rounding, or limiting complex query sequences. The mechanisms often rely on heuristic or rule-based assessments of disclosure risk, such as preventing publication of small cells that could identify individuals, or restricting queries that isolate small subpopulations, sometimes augmented with formal metrics like k-anonymity for released tables. This approach can be embedded in governance frameworks that classify types of inferences (for example, reidentification versus attribute inference) and map them to categories of controls, as in some applications of the NIST Privacy Framework. [ijirst](http://www.ijirst.org/articles/IJIRSTV6I6015.pdf)

**Literature evidence.** A recent PETS paper analyzes how the NIST Privacy Framework and its mapped NIST SP 800-53 controls address inference risks, distinguishing reidentification inferences (associating anonymised data with identities) from operational inferences (inferring sensitive attributes), and assessing control effectiveness across case studies. The analysis notes that traditional k-anonymity-based de-identification can be degraded by subsequent processing or linkage and that differential privacy techniques can overcome some limitations of quasi-identifier based SDC, although integration into existing control frameworks is still in progress. Article 29 Working Party’s Opinion 05/2014 similarly reviews anonymisation techniques, including various SDC methods, and argues that they must be applied with explicit regard to residual identification risks, attack models, and intended data uses to achieve effective anonymisation. [ec.europa](https://ec.europa.eu/justice/article-29/documentation/opinion-recommendation/files/2014/wp216_en.pdf)

**Implementations & benchmarks.** National statistical institutes and other data custodians have long implemented SDC strategies combining access policies, query logging, and output checking, although many details reside in internal documentation; available descriptions report practices such as threshold rules, dominance rules, and controlled rounding for tabular outputs. Some modern systems combine SDC with formal DP mechanisms, such as DP histograms under continual observation, providing error bounds and privacy accounting for certain query classes while retaining heuristic controls for others, though comprehensive public benchmarks are relatively sparse. Evaluations of SDC effectiveness often occur indirectly, via demonstrations of reidentification attacks on insufficiently protected releases and comparative studies showing how DP or stronger models reduce these vulnerabilities. [arxiv](http://arxiv.org/pdf/2306.10428.pdf)

**Strengths & limitations.** Query-oriented SDC aligns with institutional practices and regulatory emphasis on case-by-case risk assessments, allowing nuanced policies tailored to specific data collections and user communities, and can be implemented incrementally without rewriting entire analytics pipelines. Nonetheless, its heuristic nature and lack of composable guarantees can leave systems vulnerable to sophisticated or unanticipated attacks, and managing cumulative risk across many queries, users, and external data sources remains difficult without formal accounting, particularly in high-dimensional settings. These limitations have motivated increasing interest in integrating DP-like formalism into SDC frameworks, but empirical evidence on hybrid systems’ long-term performance and governance remains limited. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/1906.01337)

### 4.8 GDPR-oriented privacy engineering and pseudonymisation frameworks (Approach H)

**Theory & mechanism.** GDPR-oriented privacy engineering frameworks conceptualize privacy protection in terms of risk reduction relative to legal notions of identifiability and reasonable effort, operationalized through principles such as data protection by design and by default (Article 25), security of processing (Article 32), and the distinction between anonymisation and pseudonymisation. Pseudonymisation is defined as processing that renders personal data no longer attributable to a specific data subject without additional information kept separately and protected, thereby acting as a safeguard but not removing data from GDPR’s scope, whereas anonymisation aims at irreversibility in practice, considering all means reasonably likely to be used for reidentification. Engineering methodologies translate these principles into technical and organizational measures, including PETs, minimization patterns, access controls, and risk assessment procedures such as Data Protection Impact Assessments. [gdpr-text](https://gdpr-text.com/fr/read/article-25/)

**Literature evidence.** Article 29 Working Party Opinion 05/2014 analyzes the effectiveness and limits of anonymisation techniques, emphasizing that many outputs marketed as anonymised are in fact only pseudonymised, and that anonymisation must be engineered with explicit context and attack models in mind, rather than applied as a generic tool. ENISA’s reports on data protection engineering and pseudonymisation techniques further elaborate on data protection goals derived from GDPR principles, propose technical paradigms for data sharing that integrate advanced cryptographic and pseudonymisation schemes, and discuss their role in demonstrating compliance and reducing inference risks. The NIST Privacy Framework, while jurisdictionally neutral, is explicitly presented as a tool for improving privacy through enterprise risk management and is noted as potentially helpful for demonstrating compliance with laws such as GDPR by structuring privacy outcomes, profiles, and implementation tiers. [secureprivacy](https://secureprivacy.ai/blog/nist-privacy-framework)

**Implementations & benchmarks.** EDPB Guidelines on pseudonymisation and ENISA pseudonymisation guidance provide detailed criteria and examples for effective pseudonymisation, including requirements on separation and protection of additional information, unlinkability across contexts, and security controls, although quantitative benchmarks are largely qualitative or case-based rather than statistical. ENISA’s data protection engineering report presents use cases where technical measures such as attribute-based encryption, proxy re-encryption, and user-controlled data sharing architectures are evaluated in terms of their ability to support GDPR principles like intervenability, transparency, and data minimization, using scenario-based analysis rather than formal error metrics. NIST’s Privacy Framework version 1.0 and its subsequent roadmap outline Core Functions, Categories, and Subcategories, along with Profiles and Implementation Tiers, to help organizations prioritize privacy outcomes and manage risks, but they do not prescribe specific technologies, instead relying on organizations to map PETs and controls into the framework. [petsymposium](https://petsymposium.org/popets/2024/popets-2024-0013.pdf)

**Strengths & limitations.** GDPR-oriented engineering frameworks provide a bridge between formal or semi-formal technical guarantees and legal standards, helping practitioners reason about when techniques such as pseudonymisation, anonymisation, DP, or cryptographic PETs may contribute to compliance and risk reduction. However, they deliberately remain technology-agnostic and context-dependent, which means they do not in themselves offer quantitative guarantees or clear thresholds for when data cease to be personal, and substantial interpretive and evidentiary gaps remain in mapping mathematical privacy parameters (for example, \(\epsilon\)) to legal notions like “reasonably likely” reidentification. Evidence on how organizations actually integrate formal privacy technologies into GDPR compliance programs is still emerging, with case studies and guidance suggesting approaches but not yet forming a consolidated, empirically grounded body of practice. [pwc](https://www.pwc.lu/en/general-data-protection/docs/pwc-anonymisation-and-pseudonymisation.pdf)

## 6. Open Problems & Gaps

- Formalizing links between legal standards and mathematical guarantees. There is currently no widely accepted mapping between parameters such as \(\epsilon\), k, l, or t, and GDPR’s “reasonably likely” reidentification threshold, leaving controllers without principled methods to argue that particular formal configurations achieve anonymisation rather than pseudonymisation, especially in the presence of rich external data and evolving attack capabilities. [edpb.europa](https://www.edpb.europa.eu/system/files/2025-01/edpb_guidelines_202501_pseudonymisation_en.pdf)

- Compositional risk across heterogeneous mechanisms. Many real systems combine syntactic anonymity, DP mechanisms, SDC rules, and cryptographic PETs, yet systematic theories and empirical studies of how risks compose across such heterogeneous protections, especially over time and across data sources, remain sparse, complicating both engineering and accountability. [paper.ijcsns](http://paper.ijcsns.org/07_book/201712/20171225.pdf)

- Fairness and subgroup-specific impacts under formal privacy. Evidence suggests that LDP and DP synthetic data can disproportionately degrade utility for minority or low-density subgroups, and that generative models may leak more information about rare or underrepresented individuals, but formal frameworks that jointly reason about privacy and fairness at subgroup level are still in early stages. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.02971v1)

- Robust evaluation methodologies for synthetic data privacy. While membership inference and membership collision attacks are widely used, standardized benchmarks, threat models, and metrics for evaluating synthetic data privacy (both DP and non-DP) are not yet fully consolidated, which hampers comparison across methods and the development of reliable certification or labeling schemes. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.03668v1)

- Operationalizing privacy engineering frameworks in organizations. Guidance such as ENISA reports, EDPB guidelines, and the NIST Privacy Framework provide high-level paradigms, but empirical research on how organizations actually adopt, tailor, and sustain privacy engineering practices, including PET selection, monitoring, and incident response, remains limited, particularly outside a small set of early adopters. [nist](https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2020/01/nist-releases-version-10-privacy-framework)

- Longitudinal and cross-context privacy accounting. Formal models like DP provide composition theorems, but real individuals participate in many datasets and systems over long periods, raising open questions about how to aggregate or bound overall privacy loss across contexts, institutions, and jurisdictions, especially given heterogeneous mechanisms and legal obligations. [arxiv](http://arxiv.org/pdf/2409.11680.pdf)

## 7. Conclusion

This survey has documented a diverse landscape of privacy engineering approaches, ranging from syntactic anonymity models and differential privacy to synthetic data methods, cryptographic computation, query-based disclosure control, and GDPR-oriented risk management frameworks, each anchored in distinct conceptions of what it means to protect individuals in data analysis. The analysis reveals that formal methods like central and local differential privacy offer strong, composable guarantees at the cost of increased complexity and potential utility degradation, whereas heuristic or framework-based approaches more closely match institutional and regulatory practices but rely on contextual judgments and evolving attack models rather than fixed mathematical bounds. [ec.europa](https://ec.europa.eu/justice/article-29/documentation/opinion-recommendation/files/2014/wp216_en.pdf)

Across the approaches, three structural trade-offs emerge as central. First, there is a balance between trust assumptions and statistical efficiency, with LDP reducing trust in curators at the expense of higher variance compared with central DP, and syntactic anonymity trading composability for intuitive notions of protection in static releases. Second, there is tension between individual-level guarantees and group-level outcomes, as illustrated by DP synthetic data and LDP where underrepresented groups may face distinct privacy-utility frontiers despite uniform formal guarantees. Third, there is ongoing negotiation between mathematically precise guarantees and legally framed notions of identifiability and reasonableness, with GDPR-oriented frameworks increasingly engaging with formal privacy technologies while retaining a fundamentally risk-based and context-sensitive perspective. [petsymposium](https://petsymposium.org/popets/2018/popets-2018-0022.pdf)
