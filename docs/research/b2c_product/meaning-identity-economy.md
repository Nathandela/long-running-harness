# The Meaning & Identity Economy: Consumer Product Opportunities

*2026-03-18*

---

## Abstract

The post-industrial consumer landscape has undergone a fundamental reorientation: as material subsistence needs are broadly satisfied across wealthy nations, the primary driver of consumption has shifted toward the pursuit of meaning, self-definition, and existential coherence. This paper surveys the theoretical foundations and empirical evidence underpinning what scholars are beginning to call the "Meaning and Identity Economy" — a constellation of markets in which products succeed insofar as they help consumers answer the questions "Who am I?" and "Why does any of this matter?" Drawing on post-materialist political sociology (Inglehart), narrative psychology (McAdams), terror management theory (Greenberg, Solomon, and Pyszczynski), the extended-self framework (Belk), and the experience economy model (Pine and Gilmore), the paper constructs a theoretical architecture capable of explaining both the trajectory of contemporary consumption and the design space available to product builders.

The second focus of the paper is empirical: we map the meaning and identity economy across six product categories — quantified-self devices and apps, story-based platforms, secular spirituality and wellness products, identity-signaling goods, personal growth and learning platforms, and community-belonging networks — and survey the market-size benchmarks, growth rates, and canonical exemplars within each. The global wellness economy reached $6.8 trillion in 2024 and is projected to hit $9.8 trillion by 2029; the personal development market is valued at $58.3 billion with a 6.4% CAGR through 2035; spiritual wellness apps are growing at 14.6% CAGR; and the wearable fitness-tracking segment reached $62.9 billion in 2024. These are not niche curiosities but among the fastest-growing sectors in the global economy.

The paper closes by identifying open problems and structural tensions — the authenticity paradox, the risk of commodifying meaning itself, the emergence of algorithmically constructed identity, and the difficulty of measuring meaningfulness — that define the frontier for both scholarship and product innovation. The synthesis presented is descriptive and analytical rather than prescriptive: the goal is to equip researchers and strategists with a rigorous conceptual map of a domain that is poorly bounded but economically consequential.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Problem Statement

In 1943, Abraham Maslow published "A Theory of Human Motivation," proposing a hierarchical ordering of human needs from physiological survival through safety, belonging, esteem, and finally self-actualization. The hierarchy was not originally conceived as a theory of consumer markets, but it has become the implicit architectural blueprint for understanding why affluent consumers spend money in ways that seem economically irrational: they pay premium prices for experiences rather than objects, for community rather than convenience, for tools that help them understand themselves rather than purely instrumental goods.

The puzzle, stated precisely, is this: once material subsistence is reliably satisfied, what organizes the remaining space of consumption? The empirical answer — visible across decades of World Values Survey data, market-size trends, and product-category growth rates — is that meaning and identity move to the center. People spend to construct and signal who they are, to anchor themselves in narratives that give coherence to their lives, to participate in communities that confer belonging, and to encounter experiences that feel significant. The commercial infrastructure that serves these needs constitutes what this paper terms the "Meaning and Identity Economy."

### 1.2 Scope and Key Definitions

**Meaning economy:** The aggregate of markets in which the primary value proposition delivered to the consumer is existential or narrative — products help consumers answer questions of purpose, identity, self-understanding, and significance rather than primarily solving logistical problems.

**Post-material values:** Following Ronald Inglehart (1977, 1997), values that prioritize self-expression, quality of life, autonomy, and subjective well-being over physical and economic security. Post-material values become dominant as generational cohorts come of age in conditions of reliable material abundance.

**Narrative identity:** Following Dan McAdams (1985, 2001), the internalized, evolving story that a person constructs about their own life, giving it unity, purpose, and continuity across time. Products that participate in this story — by providing its raw material, its audience, or its medium — are identity-relevant goods.

**Extended self:** Following Russell Belk (1988, 2013), the proposition that possessions — physical and increasingly digital — are incorporated into the self-concept such that ownership constitutes a form of being. The boundaries of the self extend to what one owns, controls, and identifies with.

**Quantified self:** Following Gary Wolf and Kevin Kelly's 2007 concept, the practice of using technology to acquire data about oneself — biological, behavioral, environmental — as a basis for self-knowledge, self-optimization, and identity expression.

This paper does not attempt to predict which specific products will succeed; it maps the territory and theoretical levers through which meaning-economy products function or fail.

## 2. Theoretical Foundations

### 2.1 Maslow's Hierarchy and the Satiation of Material Needs

Abraham Maslow's five-tier model — physiological needs, safety, love and belonging, esteem, and self-actualization — implies a natural progression of motivational priority as lower-order needs are met. In the context of affluent post-industrial societies, this progression has become literal: the median household in a high-income country has reliable access to food, shelter, and physical safety, which frees cognitive and economic resources for higher-order pursuits.

Contemporary research both supports and complicates the strict hierarchical reading. Tay and Diener (2011), analyzing 123 countries and 60,865 participants, confirmed that universal human needs exist but do not follow Maslow's strict sequential ordering — people satisfy belonging and esteem needs even when some safety needs remain unmet. For consumer markets, the practical implication is more tractable: across the broad middle and upper strata of wealthy economies, physiological and safety needs are largely satisfied, and the competitive differentiation in product markets increasingly plays out at the levels of esteem, belonging, and self-actualization.

Marketing research confirms the commercial salience of this observation: 82% of consumers report wanting brands to help them achieve personal goals, and purchase decisions are increasingly linked to identity formation rather than purely functional need satisfaction.

### 2.2 Inglehart's Post-Materialism and the World Values Survey

Ronald Inglehart's post-materialism thesis, developed in *The Silent Revolution* (1977) and elaborated through the World Values Survey — conducted across 100+ countries since 1981 — provides the most empirically grounded account of the macro-level shift toward meaning-oriented consumption.

Inglehart's core claim is a socialization hypothesis: individuals prioritize the values that were scarce during their formative years. Cohorts raised in conditions of physical security and material abundance internalize post-material values — self-expression, quality of life, environmental concern, autonomy, aesthetic satisfaction — rather than material values oriented toward economic and physical security. Since formative values are remarkably stable across the life course, the gradual generational replacement of older (more materialist) cohorts by younger (more post-materialist) cohorts produces a secular, population-level shift toward post-material priorities in wealthy nations.

The commercial implications are direct. Post-materialist consumers prefer experiences over possessions, ethically sourced over merely functional goods, products that express individual identity over mass-market commodities, and brands whose values align with their own. The theory predicts that as median wealth rises in a society, the market for meaning-economy products expands structurally — not as a marketing niche, but as the new center of gravity for consumer spending. This explains the simultaneous growth of premium wellness, purpose-driven brands, the experience economy, and identity-signaling platforms.

A 2024 study using Inglehart's post-materialist index confirmed that post-materialist orientation significantly predicts environmentally conscious consumer behavior even under inflationary pressure — suggesting that post-material values are resilient to economic headwinds once internalized.

### 2.3 Viktor Frankl and the Will to Meaning

Viktor Frankl's logotherapy — developed partly through his experience in Nazi concentration camps and formalized in *Man's Search for Meaning* (1946) — posits that the primary motivational force in human psychology is not pleasure (Freud) or power (Adler) but the will to find meaning. The "existential vacuum," Frankl's term for the experience of meaninglessness, is not a clinical pathology but a widely distributed feature of modern life, particularly in societies that have solved the problem of material scarcity but not the problem of purpose.

Research consistently confirms that perceived meaning correlates strongly with life satisfaction, resilience, and psychological well-being, while its absence predicts depression, anxiety, and maladaptive coping. One study found that meaningfulness accounted for 62% of the variance in work engagement. The implication for consumer markets: products that credibly deliver a sense of meaning or purpose — however constructed — meet a genuine and deep psychological need that is structurally unmet by affluence alone.

Frankl's framework suggests why self-help, wellness, and personal development markets are not merely luxury indulgences but responses to a genuine existential condition of modern life. The "meaning market" is not manufactured by advertising; it responds to a structural psychological demand created by post-material conditions.

### 2.4 McAdams' Narrative Identity Theory

Dan McAdams (1985, 2001, 2013) proposed that identity in adulthood is fundamentally narrative: people construct a personal myth — an internalized, evolving story with characters, settings, themes, and plot — that gives their life unity and purpose. This "life story" is not merely a biographical record but the generative structure through which the self is constituted and communicated.

The narrative identity framework has direct implications for consumer behavior. Products that become props, chapters, or audiences in a person's life story achieve a degree of psychological integration that purely functional goods cannot. The runner who posts a Strava activity is not merely logging data — they are authoring a chapter in a story of discipline and physical transformation. The reader whose Goodreads profile shows three hundred annotated classics is constructing a narrative self as an intellectual. The Duolingo learner who maintains a 500-day streak has woven a thread of persistence into their self-narrative.

McAdams identifies "redemption sequences" — narratives in which suffering or stagnation transforms into growth — as particularly psychologically potent. Products that position themselves within such arcs (fitness journeys, sobriety tracking, language acquisition) tap the deepest structure of how people organize their sense of self across time.

### 2.5 Russell Belk's Extended Self Theory

Belk's 1988 paper "Possessions and the Extended Self" introduced one of the most influential constructs in consumer research: the proposition that people incorporate possessions into their self-concept such that "we are what we have and what we have is us." Possessions are not merely instrumental tools but constitute the self, extending it beyond the biological boundary of the body.

In a 2013 update, Belk extended the framework to the digital domain: avatars, social media profiles, digital libraries, curated playlists, and online identities are incorporated into the extended self just as physical possessions are, but with distinctive properties — they are dematerialized, sharable, co-constructed, and support distributed memory. A 2025 review in *SAGE Open* identified five dimensions of digital extended self: sharing, dematerialization, re-embodiment, co-construction, and distributed memory — with datafication proposed as a sixth.

The commercial implication is that products which are deeply integrated into a consumer's extended self achieve exceptional switching costs and loyalty. The iPhone is not just a phone; the Kindle library is not just content; the Oura ring is not just a device. They are constituents of the self, and abandoning them feels like self-diminishment.

### 2.6 Terror Management Theory

Terror Management Theory (TMT), developed by Greenberg, Solomon, and Pyszczynski (1986) based on Becker's *The Denial of Death*, proposes that awareness of mortality generates existential terror that humans manage through two anxiety buffers: self-esteem (believing oneself is a valued participant in a meaningful universe) and cultural worldview (a shared symbolic system that gives life meaning and promises some form of continuity beyond biological death).

A 2025 meta-analysis in the *International Journal of Consumer Studies* synthesized decades of TMT research in consumer contexts, finding that mortality salience — reminders of death — consistently affects consumer responses across three categories: "I want more" responses (increased materialism, conspicuous consumption, brand preference), "protect my culture" responses (preference for familiar domestic brands), and "pro-social" responses (charitable giving, community orientation).

The framework explains why luxury goods, brand loyalty, and status signaling are not mere vanity but perform a deep psychological function: they are mortality buffers, tools for constructing the sense that one matters and will be remembered. It also explains the growth of legacy-oriented products — life-history books, memorial platforms, heritage brands — that explicitly promise forms of symbolic continuity.

## 4. Analysis

### 4.1 Post-Material Values and the Meaning Economy

#### Theory and Mechanism

Inglehart's post-materialism thesis predicts a secular, generational shift in consumer priorities as formative affluence becomes widespread. The mechanism is a scarcity hypothesis operating at the level of lifetime values formation: what was scarce during childhood becomes the object of adult pursuit. For cohorts raised in material abundance (broadly, those born in wealthy countries after 1960), the scarce goods are existential rather than economic — meaning, autonomy, authentic self-expression, aesthetic experience, and community of genuine choice.

The World Values Survey documents this shift empirically across five waves from 1981 to the present. The "Emancipative values" index — measuring emphasis on autonomy, equality, and self-expression — has risen consistently across wealthy nations and is strongly correlated with GDP per capita. Critically, the shift is not cyclical but cohort-driven: it persists even under economic stress because formative values are sticky.

#### Literature Evidence

Post-materialist values predict experience preferences over material goods, ethical consumption, willingness-to-pay for authenticity, community participation, and resistance to mass-market standardization. The fashion sustainability literature shows that post-materialist orientation predicts preference for slow fashion, secondhand markets (Depop grew GMS 31.6% YoY to $789 million in 2024), and artisanal goods.

A 2024 MDPI study confirmed that post-materialist index scores significantly predict pro-environmental consumer behaviors even controlling for income and demographics — demonstrating that the value orientation is a genuine behavioral predictor, not merely an aspiration.

#### Consumer Product Implications and Benchmarks

The post-material shift creates structural demand for:
- Products that express individual values (ethical, aesthetic, social)
- Experiences that are memorable rather than merely useful
- Brands with coherent purpose beyond profit
- Community and co-creation over mass consumption

The experience economy (Pine and Gilmore, 1998/2020) is the commercial operationalization of this shift. Experiences function as inputs to the life story; they are sought not for their functional output but for their narrative and identity value. The "experience-as-product" model now extends from tourism into fitness (Peloton, SoulCycle), education (MasterClass), dining (tasting menus as events), and wellness (retreat travel, which grew 19.5% annually from 2019-2024 per the Global Wellness Institute).

#### Strengths and Limitations

Inglehart's theory has significant empirical support across cultural contexts but has attracted criticism. The strict hierarchy of materialist-to-post-materialist progression is contested — studies show post-material values can coexist with material insecurity (Welzel, 2013). The theory is also primarily a macro-level sociological account; its translation to individual-level consumer psychology requires supplementary frameworks. Finally, the model was developed in the global North and its applicability to rapidly industrializing economies remains contested.

### 4.2 The Quantified Self Movement

#### Theory and Mechanism

The quantified self movement — catalyzed by Gary Wolf and Kevin Kelly's 2007 formulation — is built on the proposition that personal data, sufficiently detailed and well-visualized, constitutes a form of self-knowledge. The product category sits at the intersection of the extended self (the data stream is part of me), narrative identity (my biometric history is a chapter in my health story), and the cultural premium placed on scientific self-understanding.

The psychological mechanism is self-concept clarity: people with higher self-concept clarity report greater life satisfaction, lower anxiety, and more effective goal pursuit. Quantified-self products offer an instrumental path to clarity — they make the self legible, measurable, and improvable.

#### Literature Evidence

A 2025 meta-analysis in *Psychology & Marketing* (Jain et al.) reviewed the literature on self-quantification and consumer well-being. Key findings: self-quantification products improve self-monitoring effectiveness and increase motivation for health behaviors, but the relationship is moderated by goal orientation and autonomy — users who feel the data is self-chosen rather than externally mandated show stronger well-being outcomes. A Pew Research survey found approximately 69% of U.S. adults track at least one health indicator, with 60% tracking weight, diet, or exercise.

The "quantified self as mirror" model explains the premium pricing tolerance in this category: a product that functions as a reliable and revealing mirror of the self creates unique value that cannot be substituted by lower-priced alternatives.

#### Consumer Product Implications and Benchmarks

**Market size:** The global wearable fitness trackers market reached $62.9 billion in 2024, projected to reach $352 billion by 2033 (CAGR 18.5%). The broader wearables market was estimated at $179.8 billion in 2024. The U.S. led with approximately $46.8 billion in 2024.

**Key products:**
- **Oura Ring:** Focused on readiness, sleep architecture, and HRV; differentiated by its ring form factor and clinical research partnerships. Positions itself explicitly as a tool for self-understanding rather than fitness gamification.
- **WHOOP:** Subscription model at ~$239/year; primarily targets performance athletes and high-achievers. Identity signaling (the band itself is a status marker in gyms) is as important as the data.
- **Apple Watch / Health:** Mass-market entrant that normalized continuous biometric tracking. As of 2024, Apple Watch is the world's best-selling watch brand; its health platform serves as an identity anchor for the Apple ecosystem.
- **Continuous glucose monitors (CGMs):** Abbott's Lingo (FDA approved June 2024) and Dexcom's Stelo (OTC, $89-99) launched for non-diabetic wellness consumers. The OTC CGM market reached $370.7 million in 2024 and is growing at 16.9% CAGR. Non-diabetic users account for 41.46% of the OTC market. Startups Levels and NutriSense pair CGM hardware with subscription analytics.
- **Journaling apps:** Day One, Rosebud (AI-assisted reflection), and similar platforms quantify the qualitative — turning introspective practice into archived, searchable personal data.

#### Strengths and Limitations

The quantified self category is structurally advantaged by the health-anxiety post-pandemic consumer environment, improving sensor miniaturization, and the identity-premium pricing model. Key limitations: data anxiety (surveillance concerns, insurance pricing fears), the "metrics fixation" problem (optimizing measured variables at the expense of unmeasured well-being), and the paradox of self-tracking addiction — some research suggests tracking can undermine intrinsic motivation for exercise by shifting the locus of control from internal experience to external data.

### 4.3 Narrative Psychology and Story-Based Products

#### Theory and Mechanism

McAdams' narrative identity theory holds that humans are storytelling animals who construct a personal myth to give their lives unity and purpose. This story is not merely autobiographical record but generative structure — it shapes which experiences are noticed, which are remembered, and how they are interpreted. Bruner (1990) made the complementary argument that the narrative mode of cognition is distinct from the logical-scientific mode and is primary for organizing social and personal meaning.

The consumer product implication is that products which are incorporated into a person's life narrative — as the medium of the story, its archive, its audience, or its social validation system — achieve a distinctive form of value that is psychologically integrative rather than merely functional.

#### Literature Evidence

Research in *Journal of the Association for Consumer Research* (2024) synthesized evidence on how consumers use and respond to narratives, finding that narrative transportation (psychological immersion in a story) increases product evaluation, brand trust, and purchase intention beyond the effects of argument quality. Woodside et al. (2008) in *Psychology & Marketing* established that consumer-brand storytelling creates identification (the consumer sees themselves in the brand's story) that drives loyalty outcomes.

The concept of the "redemption sequence" (McAdams) — in which a story of suffering or struggle transforms into growth — is commercially potent: fitness apps, sobriety trackers, language learning platforms, and weight loss programs all deploy redemption narrative structures in their product design and marketing.

#### Consumer Product Implications and Benchmarks

**Strava:** 180 million users, reportedly $500 million ARR in 2025, valued at $2.2 billion. Strava does not sell fitness tracking — it sells athletic identity and social narrative. Every activity upload is a public chapter in the user's self-authored story of physical transformation. The "Kudos" system (Strava's like equivalent) functions as social validation of the narrative. "If it's not on Strava, it didn't happen" — the platform's unofficial motto — reveals the narrative logic: experience becomes real through its documentation and sharing.

**Letterboxd:** Film-logging platform that grew from 1.8 million users in 2020 to 17 million by end of 2024, with members writing 96.4 million reviews and marking 701 million films watched. A Letterboxd profile curated toward obscure international cinema or classical Hollywood is a detailed identity statement — a public-facing chapter of the user's self-narrative as a person of refined taste. The platform explicitly enables what its users describe as "cultural identity construction."

**Goodreads:** Amazon-owned platform with 150+ million members; despite product stagnation, it persists because the "books read" list is one of the most potent identity signals available to middle-class consumers. Reading history communicates intellectual identity, values, and community affiliation in ways that few other consumption records do.

**Day One / journaling apps:** Personal journaling platforms that archive private self-narrative. Day One, with 25+ million entries from millions of users, positions itself as a "lifelong journal." Rosebud (AI-assisted journaling) uses LLM technology to facilitate guided self-reflection, generating a structured personal narrative from daily inputs.

**BeReal:** The anti-performance social platform, positioned against the curated identity of Instagram. BeReal's product logic is narrative authenticity — sharing an unfiltered, dual-camera moment rather than a polished self-representation. Reached 73+ million monthly active users by 2023, though growth subsequently moderated.

#### Strengths and Limitations

Story-based products benefit from high switching costs (narrative archive lock-in), strong community network effects, and deep psychological integration. Limitations include the paradox of digital permanence — the anxiety of a searchable, permanent record of one's past self — and the tension between authentic self-narration and social performance. Users increasingly curate even on "authentic" platforms, undermining the narrative authenticity promise.

### 4.4 Secular Spirituality and the Wellness Economy

#### Theory and Mechanism

The secular spirituality category occupies the space opened by the decline of institutional religious participation in wealthy nations. In the United States, "nones" (people claiming no religious affiliation) grew from 16% of adults in 2007 to 28% by 2023 (Pew Research). The psychological needs met by religion — transcendence, community, ritual, existential anchoring, moral framework — do not disappear with institutional disaffiliation; they migrate to secular substitutes.

This is the Frankl problem at the population scale: the existential vacuum created by post-religious modernity generates genuine demand for products that provide meaning, ritual, transcendence, and community. The wellness industry, the meditation-app market, the astrology revival, and the crystal economy are all, in part, secular spirituality markets.

The mechanism draws on TMT: without a culturally shared symbolic immortality project (religion), individuals construct personal anxiety buffers — wellness practices, philosophical frameworks, community memberships — that provide existential grounding.

#### Literature Evidence

The Global Wellness Institute's 2025 Monitor documented a $6.8 trillion wellness economy in 2024, growing at 7.9% annually, projected to reach $9.8 trillion by 2029. Mental wellness is the fastest-growing sub-sector within wellness (12.4% CAGR, 2019-2024), followed by wellness real estate (19.5% CAGR). The wellness economy now comprises 6.1% of global GDP and exceeds the pharmaceutical industry ($1.8 trillion) by nearly four times.

Research confirms that more than 68% of consumers globally engage in at least one form of spiritual product or service participation, and that over 60% of millennials and Gen Z actively use spiritual tools for stress relief (spiritual wellness apps market data, 2024).

#### Consumer Product Implications and Benchmarks

**Meditation and mindfulness apps:**
- **Calm:** Approximately 4 million paid subscribers in 2024 (declining to ~3.5M by 2025). Valued at $2 billion at peak.
- **Headspace:** $348.4 million revenue in 2024; approximately 2.3 million paid subscribers; 100 million total customers lifetime. Operates on a B2B2C model — employers purchase as employee wellness benefits.
- **Hallow:** Catholic prayer app with 18+ million downloads as of November 2024; the fastest-growing spiritual app in the App Store during 2023-2024. Demonstrates demand for digitized religious practice, not only secular alternatives.

The spiritual wellness apps market overall was valued at $2.16 billion in 2024 and is projected to reach $7.31 billion by 2033 (CAGR 14.63%). Meditation and mindfulness apps command 42.14% of this market; paid subscriptions account for 62.94% of revenue.

**Astrology apps:**
- **Co-Star:** 30+ million global users; 151,000+ downloads in September 2024 alone; approximately $400K monthly revenue. Gen Z platform.
- **The Pattern:** ~40,000 monthly downloads in 2024; personality-based readings produce 2-4x longer session durations than traditional horoscope apps.
- **Market size:** Global astrology app market reached $3.94 billion in 2024, with 120+ million monthly active users across platforms.

The astrology revival is not primarily a belief phenomenon — it is an identity and community phenomenon. Co-Star's Sun-Moon-Rising identity schema functions as a secular personality taxonomy (analogous to Myers-Briggs or Enneagram) that users incorporate into self-concept and social communication.

**Physical spiritual/wellness products:**
The spiritual and wellness products market (crystals, essential oils, incense, meditation aids) was valued at $4.2 billion in 2023, projected to reach $9.6 billion by 2034 (CAGR 8.0%). The broader spiritual products and services market stands at $186.5 billion in 2025. The global spiritual services market is valued at $392 billion in 2025.

**Wellness retreats and experiences:** Wellness real estate and retreat travel grew 19.5% annually from 2019-2024, driven by demand for immersive wellness experiences. Per-capita wellness spending in North America ($6,029) vastly exceeds Europe ($1,876) and Asia ($471), reflecting the post-material affluence differential.

#### Strengths and Limitations

The secular spirituality category benefits from the structural tailwind of institutional religious decline and the rising prevalence of anxiety, loneliness, and existential search among younger cohorts. Key tensions: the authenticity paradox (wellness brands appropriating sacred or traditional practices risk cultural extraction and consumer backlash); the medicalization problem (wellness claims require clinical substantiation at higher product price points); and market saturation risk in meditation apps specifically, where subscriber growth at Calm and Headspace slowed in 2024-2025.

### 4.5 Identity Signaling and Self-Expression Products

#### Theory and Mechanism

Identity signaling through consumption is as old as consumer culture itself, but the mechanism is significantly theorized by Bourdieu's cultural capital framework (1984) — taste in consumption communicates social position and cultural sophistication — and by Belk's extended self. The contemporary post-material twist is a shift from signaling through quantity and expense to signaling through specificity and authenticity. It is no longer sufficient to own expensive goods; the identity signal requires that the goods be specific, rare, personally meaningful, and aligned with values.

Mintel's 2025 *US Expressions of Identity* report documents that half of all consumers seek products that reflect their unique identity, while 65% feel society accepts who they truly are — creating an ongoing market for expanded identity expression. Brands can create deep emotional connection by supporting authentic self-expression and personal growth journeys.

#### Literature Evidence

Consumer identity theory (Kleine, Kleine, and Kernan, 1993) established that products are used to confirm or establish desired identity claims. Digital extension (Belk, 2013) added that virtual possessions and online representations are equally constitutive of the self. Research on NFT ownership (AbedRabbo et al., 2025, *Journal of Consumer Behaviour*) found that digital luxury goods function as identity expression vehicles through scarcity, exclusivity, and community membership signals — directly analogous to physical luxury.

Authenticization theory (Plesa, 2023, *Cultural Sociology*) provides the critical counterpoint: neoliberal capitalism has commodified authenticity itself, transforming it into a marketing strategy that undermines its original function. The paradox is structural: the more consumers value authentic self-expression, the larger the commercial infrastructure of "authenticity signaling" products becomes, and the more authenticity loses its claim to genuine distinctiveness.

#### Consumer Product Implications and Benchmarks

**Depop:** Resale and vintage fashion platform with 45+ million registered users, 5 million active buyers, 2.5 million active sellers. GMS grew 31.6% YoY to $789 million in 2024. Depop's identity logic is peer-to-peer curation as self-expression — each storefront is a curated identity statement as much as a commercial operation. The platform disproportionately serves Gen Z consumers who articulate their fashion choices as value alignment (sustainability, individuality, anti-fast-fashion) and community belonging.

**Niche and luxury fashion:** The "niche fashion" segment — independent designers, direct-to-consumer labels, heritage brands with cultural specificity — is structurally advantaged by post-material values because it provides authentic distinctiveness rather than mass-market conformity. Mintel notes the trend toward hyper-segmentation as brands respond to consumer demand for identity-specific products.

**Avatar economies and digital fashion:** In-game skins, digital fashion, and avatar accessories represent the extension of identity signaling into virtual space. The extended self framework (Belk) predicts that digital possessions are psychologically equivalent to physical ones — digital identity signaling is not lesser but different. The broader NFT and digital collectibles market, despite price volatility, represents a genuine identity-economy product category in which scarcity, community membership, and aesthetic alignment are the primary value propositions.

**Self-expression and the global self-improvement market:** The global Self-Improvement Products and Services market is expected to reach USD 111.6 billion by 2033 (CAGR 6.3% from 2024-2033). The US self-improvement market was valued at $16.5 billion in 2024.

#### Strengths and Limitations

The identity signaling category is durable because the underlying need — communicating who one is to oneself and others — is fundamental. The critical limitation is the authenticity treadmill: as identity signals become mainstream, their signaling value dissipates, requiring constant differentiation and novelty. This structural dynamic drives product turnover but also limits sustainable brand equity. The commodification critique (Plesa, 2023) identifies the deepest problem: markets that sell authenticity are paradoxically undermining it.

### 4.6 Personal Growth and Learning as Identity

#### Theory and Mechanism

The "identity-as-learner" category positions the consumption of educational and development content not primarily as skill acquisition but as identity construction — the ongoing project of becoming. The mechanism combines Maslow's self-actualization need (the drive to realize one's full potential), McAdams' narrative identity (the learner narrative as a coherent life story), and Dweck's growth mindset theory (the belief that intelligence and capability are developable).

The critical insight is that the value proposition of platforms like Duolingo, MasterClass, and Goodreads is partially decoupled from actual learning outcomes. A user who maintains a 500-day Duolingo streak and achieves modest conversational French has not primarily purchased language skills; they have purchased a self-narrative as a person who is disciplined, curious, and cultured. The streak itself is the identity artifact.

#### Literature Evidence

Duolingo's own data documents the psychological mechanism: the streak feature creates identity-based commitment ("I am a person who learns languages every day") that produces far stronger retention than purely skill-motivated usage. The "Duo's death" viral campaign of 2024, which generated 10 million impressions and a 5% user spike, demonstrates that the platform's primary emotional register is identity and community, not curriculum.

MasterClass research indicates that completion rates for its "courses" are significantly below typical e-learning platforms — suggesting that many subscribers derive value from access to and association with master practitioners rather than from completing structured learning programs. The product sells aspiration and identity alignment as much as knowledge.

#### Consumer Product Implications and Benchmarks

**Personal development market:** Valued at $58.3 billion in 2024 (Market Research Future), projected to reach $115.9 billion by 2035 (CAGR 6.44%). Online courses and e-learning captured approximately 37.5% of total market share in 2024; mobile apps and software tools are projected to grow at 19.6% CAGR through 2030.

**Duolingo:** Revenue grew 41% to $748 million in 2024, from $531 million in 2023. Valued at approximately $14 billion. 88.9 million daily active users as of Q4 2024. Gamification — streaks, leaderboards, XP points — converts language learning into a persistent identity performance. The brand's social media strategy, built around the anxious-owl mascot, generates identity-expressive user behavior (memes, community in-jokes) that extends brand attachment beyond the product.

**MasterClass:** Subscription platform offering courses from celebrity practitioners — actors, chefs, writers, athletes. Subscriber identity alignment with instructors (I am the kind of person who learns writing from Neil Gaiman) is the primary value proposition, supplemented by genuine educational content.

**Goodreads:** 150+ million members; the "books read" list is one of the most stable identity signals in middle-class consumer culture. Goodreads profiles function as public intellectual identity statements. The recent emergence of "BookTok" (TikTok book community) and platform extensions like Storygraph (Goodreads alternative with richer analytics) reflect the growth of reading-as-identity as a consumer segment.

**Coaching and mentoring:** The personal coaching segment dominated the personal development market with over 37% share in 2024. The coaching relationship — particularly executive and life coaching — functions as identity-formation as much as skill development. The International Coaching Federation estimated the global coaching market at $6.25 billion in 2024.

#### Strengths and Limitations

The personal growth category benefits from cultural valorization of "lifelong learning" and institutional validation of personal development as a professional credential. Key limitations: the completion-versus-aspiration gap (most users of learning platforms do not complete courses, raising questions about value delivery); identity displacement (learning-platform subscriptions may function as identity proxies that reduce motivation for the actual behavioral change they nominally support); and market fragmentation (thousands of course platforms compete on instructor brand rather than learning-science foundations).

**Cross-cutting observations:**

1. **The authenticity paradox is structural, not incidental.** Every category in the meaning economy faces the same fundamental tension: the act of selling meaning undermines its experiential character. Products that help people "be authentic" are, by virtue of their commercial existence, already contaminating the authenticity they offer. This is not a solvable design problem but a structural condition of meaning markets.

2. **Switching costs correlate with psychological integration depth.** Categories where products are most deeply incorporated into the extended self (quantified self with its data history; narrative platforms with their archive; communities with their relationships) show the highest switching costs and the most durable market positions. The implication: the path to defensible consumer product value runs through the extended self.

3. **Market growth rates are inversely correlated with maturity.** Spiritual wellness apps (14.6% CAGR) and OTC CGMs (16.9% CAGR) are in early stages of mainstream adoption; fitness trackers (18.5% CAGR) are in rapid expansion; personal development (6.44% CAGR) is a maturing market. The meaning economy is not monolithic — it has gradient structures of early, growth, and mature phases.

4. **Demographic convergence is occurring.** While Gen Z is the primary driver of astrology apps, resale fashion, and narrative social platforms, Millennial and Gen X adoption of wellness, quantified self, and personal growth products is significant and growing. The meaning economy is not generationally bounded.

5. **The community layer amplifies every category.** Products with embedded community components (Strava Clubs, Duolingo leaderboards, Depop seller communities) show stronger retention, higher LTV, and more organic growth than equivalent products without social layers. Community transforms a tool into an identity anchor.

## 6. Open Problems and Gaps

### 6.1 The Authenticity Paradox

The authenticity paradox, identified by Plesa (2023) as "authenticization," is the deepest structural tension in the meaning economy. As markets commodify the offer of authentic self-expression, the very concept of authenticity is hollowed out. The paradox has at least three distinct dimensions:

**Commercial capture of subcultures:** Subcultural identity signals — the aesthetics, practices, and language of genuine subcultures — are systematically harvested by mainstream brands. Once a signal is commodified, it loses its differentiating function for the subculture from which it emerged, forcing further differentiation in a cycle of endless cultural novelty.

**The authenticity brand as oxymoron:** A brand that markets itself as "authentic" has already undermined its claim. Authenticity is constitutionally incompatible with deliberate construction — yet meaning-economy products are necessarily constructed and marketed. This produces a class of products that are authentically experienced as authentic by consumers while being commercially manufactured as authentic by producers.

**Wellness theater:** In the wellness and spiritual category specifically, the gap between marketed transcendence and delivered experience generates a form of consumer fraud that is structurally legal but epistemically significant. Crystal energy, some functional supplement categories, and certain wellness retreats generate genuine felt benefit through placebo, ritual, and community mechanisms rather than the claimed active ingredients — raising questions about the long-term stability of these markets under scrutiny.

### 6.2 Commodification of Meaning

Frankl's warning that the "existential vacuum" cannot be filled by consumption resonates in an economy where meaning is itself a product category. The commodification critique runs as follows: if the experience of meaning requires non-commercial conditions — genuine encounter, unscripted community, actual risk and stakes — then market products can only simulate meaning at best and undermine it at worst. The meditator who completes a Headspace session has had a real relaxation experience but may not have encountered the genuine encounter with mortality and impermanence that Frankl's framework identifies as the ground of meaningful life.

This critique does not invalidate the meaning economy — it suggests that meaning products are, at best, enabling infrastructure for genuine encounters that must ultimately occur outside any product relationship. The research gap is empirical: we lack longitudinal studies tracking whether heavy consumers of meaning-economy products (meditation apps, journaling platforms, self-tracking devices) actually report higher meaningfulness over multi-year periods compared to matched controls.

### 6.3 AI-Generated Identity and the Algorithmic Self

A 2025 paper in *Frontiers in Psychology* introduced the concept of the "Algorithmic Self" — a form of digitally mediated identity in which self-awareness, preferences, and even emotional patterns are shaped through continuous feedback from AI systems. Recommendation algorithms do not passively reflect pre-existing tastes; they actively constitute them by creating feedback loops between displayed content and user behavior.

Spotify Wrapped, the canonical example, repackages listening behavior into a curated annual identity statement. Users share their Wrapped not as a music record but as an identity disclosure. The identity has been manufactured by an algorithm operating on behavioral data — yet it is experienced as authentic self-revelation.

The research question is: at what point does algorithmically constituted identity become a form of identity capture? As AI systems become more sophisticated at modeling and predicting individual preferences, the locus of identity formation may shift from the individual to the system. Products built on this dynamic (AI journals, personalized spiritual guidance apps, AI-generated learning curricula) may be identity-forming as much as identity-serving — a distinction with significant ethical and consumer-protection implications.

Algorithm awareness research (2024) found that users on TikTok who became aware of algorithmic influence reported a 0.42 decline in perceived social connectedness — suggesting that the transparency about algorithmic constitution of identity is itself destabilizing.

### 6.4 Measuring Meaningfulness

The most fundamental gap in meaning-economy research is the absence of a validated, consumer-facing measurement instrument for meaningfulness as an outcome of product experience. The literature offers several established psychometric tools — Steger's Meaning in Life Questionnaire, Ryff's psychological well-being scales, McAdams' Narrative Identity Interview — but these are research instruments, not product evaluation tools.

The commercial implication is significant: meaning-economy products are sold on the promise of meaningfulness but evaluated on metrics (downloads, DAUs, revenue, subscriber count) that are orthogonal to the promised outcome. A meditation app may grow its subscriber base while delivering no measurable increase in experienced meaningfulness; a journaling app may produce highly engaged users who never achieve the self-insight that supposedly motivates the practice.

The development of validated, practical measures of meaningfulness as a product outcome — analogous to the Net Promoter Score as a proxy for customer satisfaction — represents both a significant research gap and a potential competitive differentiator for products in this space.

### 6.5 Cross-Cultural Validity

The theoretical foundations of the meaning economy are predominantly developed in Western, educated, industrialized, rich, and democratic (WEIRD) contexts. Inglehart's post-materialism, McAdams' narrative identity, Belk's extended self, and Terror Management Theory were all developed and primarily tested in North American and Western European contexts. Their applicability to rapidly developing economies — where the transition from material to post-material values may follow different trajectories — is an important open question.

The Global Wellness Institute data reveals dramatic per-capita spending disparities: North America ($6,029) versus Asia ($471). Whether this reflects a stage-of-development difference that will converge (as Asian middle classes grow), a structural cultural difference in meaning-seeking behavior, or a methodological artifact of how wellness spending is categorized, is unresolved.

---

## 7. Conclusion

The meaning and identity economy is not a niche trend or a passing cultural moment — it is the structural consequence of affluence meeting the enduring human need for significance. As material subsistence needs are reliably satisfied across the broad middle of wealthy societies, the competitive differentiation in consumer markets has relocated to the existential level: products succeed to the extent that they help people understand themselves, tell their story, connect to something larger than themselves, and signal who they are to others.

The theoretical architecture surveyed here — Maslow's hierarchy, Inglehart's post-materialism, Frankl's will to meaning, McAdams' narrative identity, Belk's extended self, and Terror Management Theory — constitutes a coherent, empirically grounded account of why this shift is occurring and what psychological mechanisms meaning-economy products must engage. These are not decorative additions to conventional consumer psychology; they are the primary explanatory frameworks for the fastest-growing consumer sectors in the contemporary economy.

The market benchmarks document the scale: a $6.8 trillion wellness economy growing at 7.9% annually; a $58.3 billion personal development market; $62.9 billion in fitness trackers; $3.94 billion in astrology apps; $348 million from a single meditation app. These numbers are not explained by functional utility alone. They are explained by the psychological mechanisms this paper has surveyed.

The open problems — the authenticity paradox, commodification of meaning, algorithmic identity formation, and the measurement gap — define the frontier of both scholarly inquiry and product innovation. The most important question for the next generation of meaning-economy products is whether they will deepen or hollow out the genuine human experiences they invoke. That question is not resolvable within a purely commercial framework; it requires ongoing engagement with the psychological and philosophical traditions from which the meaning economy's theoretical foundations are drawn.
