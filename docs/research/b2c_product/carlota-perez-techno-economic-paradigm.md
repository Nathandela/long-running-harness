# The Carlota Perez Framework: Techno-Economic Paradigm Shifts and Consumer Product Timing

*2026-03-18*

---

## Abstract

Carlota Perez's theory of technological revolutions and financial capital provides one of the most analytically rigorous frameworks for understanding the long-wave dynamics of economic development. Drawing on five historical technological revolutions spanning 250 years — from the British Industrial Revolution to the current Information and Telecommunications age — Perez identifies a recurring two-phase structure (installation and deployment), separated by a turning point crash, through which each revolution propagates itself across the economy. The interaction between financial capital and production capital, their periodic decoupling during installation frenzies, and their forced recoupling after crashes is at the heart of the framework's explanatory power. For product strategists, the central insight is timing: the deployment phase is historically when the most durable, high-penetration consumer products emerge, built atop infrastructure installed during the preceding bubble.

This paper offers a comprehensive survey of the Perez framework, its historical evidential base, its current application to the ICT and AI technological revolution, and its implications for consumer product strategy. Section 2 establishes the foundational theory. Section 3 provides a structured taxonomy mapping each of the five revolutions to their phases and associated consumer product categories. Sections 4.1–4.5 analyze the framework's core mechanisms in depth. Section 5 synthesizes cross-cutting themes and compares the Perez model to adjacent frameworks (Rogers diffusion, Moore's chasm, S-curves). Section 6 identifies open problems and gaps — including the contested question of whether AI constitutes a new revolution or a late acceleration within the existing ICT paradigm — and Section 7 concludes.

The paper does not advocate specific investment or product decisions, but maps the theoretical terrain with enough granularity that practitioners can reason about their own positioning. The central empirical finding from historical review is consistent: deployment-phase golden ages reliably produce the largest cohort of durable consumer product categories, and those categories tend to solve real, previously unresolved problems by extending the infrastructure of the preceding installation phase into everyday life.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Problem Statement

In any era of rapid technological change, one of the most consequential questions facing product builders, investors, and policymakers is: *where are we in the cycle?* A product that would be transformative in a deployment phase may be simply too early in an installation phase. A speculative infrastructure bet that made sense during frenzy is a trap in the synergy period. Timing, in Perez's framework, is not merely tactical; it is structural.

Yet the question of current positioning within a long technological wave is notoriously difficult to answer. The agents living through any phase tend to experience it as exceptional and unprecedented. The dot-com boom of the late 1990s was experienced by participants as the permanent arrival of a new economy; the crash of 2000–2002 reframed it retrospectively as a classic installation frenzy. The 2008 financial crisis was experienced as a generalized banking crisis; the Perez lens identifies it as the second turning-point event of the ICT revolution's transition from installation to deployment. The AI boom of 2023–2025 is now being debated in analogous terms: is it the irruption of an entirely new technological revolution, or the late-frenzy acceleration of the ICT paradigm? The answers carry radically different product strategy implications.

### 1.2 Scope and Key Definitions

This paper focuses on the Perez framework as developed in *Technological Revolutions and Financial Capital: The Dynamics of Bubbles and Golden Ages* (2002) and subsequent publications, and as applied by analysts, practitioners, and academic commentators through 2025–2026.

Key definitions used throughout:

**Technological Revolution**: A powerful and highly visible cluster of new and dynamic technologies, products, and industries capable of bringing about an upheaval in the whole fabric of the economy and propelling a long-term upsurge of development (Perez, 2002). Distinguished from individual inventions by its systemic, economy-wide character.

**Techno-Economic Paradigm (TEP)**: The model and best-practice principles embodied in the core industries of a given technological revolution. A TEP is both economic (reflecting a new relative price structure) and paradigmatic (defining what counts as normal innovative behavior). The shift from mass production logic (fourth revolution) to ICT logic (fifth revolution) exemplifies the concept.

**Installation Period**: The first half of a technological revolution, consisting of irruption and frenzy phases, during which financial capital leads the build-out of new infrastructure and new industry clusters, largely decoupled from production logic.

**Deployment Period**: The second half, consisting of synergy and maturity phases, during which production capital leads the broad diffusion of the new paradigm across the economy, generating the characteristic golden age.

**Turning Point**: The crisis interval between installation and deployment — typically a financial crash — that forces the recoupling of financial and production capital under new regulatory and institutional conditions.

**Financial Capital**: Mobile, liquid capital oriented toward return on investment regardless of sector. Dominant during installation phases; drives bubble dynamics.

**Production Capital**: Capital tied to specific industries, supply chains, and productive enterprises. Dominant during deployment phases; drives real value creation.

## 2. Foundations of the Perez Framework

### 2.1 Intellectual Lineage

Perez's framework builds directly on Joseph Schumpeter's theory of creative destruction and on Nikolai Kondratieff's long-wave hypothesis. Schumpeter identified technological innovation as the primary driver of capitalist dynamics, operating through clusters of interrelated innovations that define successive technological epochs. Kondratieff documented approximately 50-year economic waves corresponding roughly to major technological transformations. Perez synthesizes and extends both, adding the specific mechanism of financial capital dynamics to explain *why* technological revolutions follow the boom-bust-boom pattern they do, rather than diffusing smoothly.

Freeman and Perez (1988) introduced the concept of the techno-economic paradigm as the connective tissue between technological change and economic organization. Each new paradigm does not simply add new products but shifts the entire logic of what constitutes best practice in production, organization, and innovation across the economy.

### 2.2 The Five Technological Revolutions

Perez identifies five great technological surges over the past 250 years, each ignited by a specific "big bang" innovation event:

1. **First Revolution — The Industrial Revolution** (Big Bang: 1771, Arkwright's Cromford Mill, England). Core technology cluster: mechanized cotton spinning, wrought iron, water-powered mills. Key infrastructure: canal networks. Paradigm logic: factory production, mechanization of craft.

2. **Second Revolution — Steam and Railways** (Big Bang: 1829, Rocket steam engine test on Liverpool-Manchester Railway, England/USA/Europe). Core cluster: steam engines, coal mining, iron and steel for rails, railway engineering. Infrastructure: national railway networks. Paradigm logic: speed, scale economies, national market integration.

3. **Third Revolution — Steel, Electricity, and Heavy Engineering** (Big Bang: 1875, Carnegie's Bessemer steel plant, Pittsburgh, USA/Germany). Core cluster: cheap steel, electricity generation and transmission, heavy engineering, chemicals. Infrastructure: electrical grids, telegraph, steel bridges. Paradigm logic: economies of scale in capital-intensive production, standardization.

4. **Fourth Revolution — Oil, Automobiles, and Mass Production** (Big Bang: 1908, Ford Model T launch, USA/Germany). Core cluster: internal combustion engine, oil, automobiles, petrochemicals, mass production techniques. Infrastructure: road networks, oil pipelines, suburbs. Paradigm logic: mass production, standardized products, Fordism, consumer society.

5. **Fifth Revolution — Information and Telecommunications (ICT)** (Big Bang: 1971, Intel microprocessor, USA/Europe/Asia). Core cluster: semiconductors, computers, software, telecommunications, internet, mobile. Infrastructure: global fiber networks, data centers, internet protocols. Paradigm logic: knowledge-intensity, networking, software-defined products, information as a productive input.

### 2.3 The Four Phases and Two Periods

Each revolution follows a characteristic two-period, four-phase structure:

**Installation Period:**

*Irruption*: The big bang event triggers explosive investment into the new technology cluster. Early industries are established; infrastructure construction begins. Venture-style financial capital rushes toward high-profit opportunities. The new technology is still expensive, crude, and largely confined to specialist adopters, but productivity gains in early adopter sectors are dramatic.

*Frenzy*: Financial capital, intoxicated by extraordinary returns in the new sector, floods in at an accelerating pace. Infrastructure buildout intensifies (railways, electrical grids, fiber optic networks). Speculation becomes detached from production fundamentals — asset prices in the revolutionary sector soar far beyond what any reasonable productivity calculation can justify. New financial instruments proliferate. The phrase "this time is different" circulates. The decoupling of financial capital from production capital reaches its maximum.

**The Turning Point (Crisis)**:

The frenzy collapses. Asset prices in the revolutionary sector crash, often catastrophically. Financial capital suffers massive losses ("evaporation of paper gains"). Political and regulatory pressure mounts to constrain speculative excess and direct investment toward productive ends. The crash itself is the mechanism by which institutional reform becomes politically possible — the Great Depression enabled the New Deal, the dot-com crash enabled early internet regulation, the 2008 crisis enabled (partially) regulatory reform of financial services. Without the turning point, the deployment phase cannot begin in earnest.

**Deployment Period:**

*Synergy*: Post-crisis, production capital takes the lead. The infrastructure built during installation (now cheap and widely available after distressed-asset prices collapse) becomes the platform for broad application development. Financial capital, humbled by bubble losses, accepts tighter coupling to productive enterprises. This is the phase of maximal societal diffusion — the technology spreads beyond its pioneer sectors into the entire economy. Positive-sum dynamics between different industries adopting the paradigm create sustained productivity growth. This is the golden age: rising real incomes, expanding consumer markets, increasing social inclusion in the benefits of the technology.

*Maturity*: Growth in the revolutionary sector decelerates. Markets become saturated. Dominant firms consolidate through mergers and acquisitions into oligopolistic structures. Financial capital, unable to find sufficient returns in mature industries, begins scanning for the next revolution. The conditions for the next irruption are being assembled.

### 2.4 Financial Capital vs. Production Capital: The Core Dynamic

The tension between financial capital and production capital is the engine of the Perez cycle. Financial capital is inherently mobile and unconstrained — it follows returns wherever they lead, regardless of whether those returns reflect genuine productive value creation. Production capital, by contrast, is "basically tied to concrete products, both by installed equipment with specific operational capabilities and by linkages in networks of suppliers, customers or distributors in particular geographic locations" (Perez, Chapter 7, *Technological Revolutions and Financial Capital*).

During irruption, this mobility is a virtue: financial capital can fund genuinely novel, high-uncertainty ventures that production-oriented firms would never back. The problem arises in frenzy, when financial capital begins generating returns through financial engineering rather than productive investment — when investment in financial innovations (derivatives, new securities structures, leverage) yields better returns than investment in real enterprises. At this point, financial capital and production capital are structurally opposed: the logic of paper gains actively undermines the logic of productive investment.

The turning point crash destroys the paper gains that sustained this opposition. What follows is not merely a return to the prior equilibrium but the establishment of new institutional rules — regulatory frameworks, social contracts, financial governance — that realign the incentives of financial capital with productive deployment. Only under these new conditions can the golden age proceed.

### 2.5 The Turning Point as Institutional Event

A critical and sometimes underappreciated element of Perez's framework is that the quality of the turning point — specifically, the quality of institutional reform it enables — determines the character of the subsequent deployment phase. A turning point that produces robust institutional reform (New Deal after 1929, Keynesian welfare state after WWII) generates an inclusive golden age with broad consumer market expansion. A turning point that produces only technocratic financial stabilization without structural reform yields a narrower, more unequal deployment phase.

This is not mechanical or inevitable: the framework explicitly holds that what follows the turning point is contingent on political choices. Perez has consistently argued that the current ICT revolution's full golden age awaits the construction of adequate institutional conditions — conditions she characterizes as "smart, green, fair and global" growth — and that without deliberate policy direction, the deployment benefits will be distributed too narrowly to constitute a true golden age.

## 4. Analysis

### 4.1 The Five Technological Revolutions: Historical Pattern

#### Theory and Mechanism

Each technological revolution, in Perez's account, begins with a cluster of interrelated innovations — not a single invention — that creates a new cost structure for production. The key dynamic is not merely the invention itself but the point at which the new technology becomes cheap enough to displace the prior cost structure across wide swaths of the economy. The 1771 date for the first revolution marks not the invention of the spinning jenny (1764) but Arkwright's factory system at Cromford — the moment the new technology became an organizational and economic reality at scale.

The mechanism of propagation operates through what Perez calls the "techno-economic paradigm": a new model of best practice that, once established, makes the prior paradigm economically obsolete. Enterprises adopting the new paradigm gain productivity advantages so large that competitors are forced to follow or face extinction. This is why technological revolutions are not merely additive: they do not simply create new industries alongside old ones, but eventually transform or displace the entire fabric of existing industries.

The paradigm shift involves changes in: (1) the relative price structure (the inputs that become cheapest define the new paradigm's logic — steam power in the second revolution, cheap steel in the third, cheap oil in the fourth, cheap computing/communication in the fifth); (2) organizational best practice (factory system, vertical integration, divisional corporation, network firm); (3) infrastructure requirements; and (4) social and institutional frameworks that must adapt to the new paradigm.

#### Historical Evidence: Deployment Consumer Booms by Revolution

**First Revolution deployment (c.1800–1820s)**: Cheap factory-made textiles (especially cotton) became the first mass-market manufactured consumer good, displacing artisan-made cloth and enabling working-class access to a wider variety of affordable clothing. This was not a dramatic consumer revolution by later standards — mass consumption required the further infrastructure of railways and mass production — but it established the principle of factory-made goods at accessible prices.

**Second Revolution deployment (c.1850s–1870s)**: The buildout of railway networks transformed consumer experience across multiple dimensions simultaneously. Mass passenger rail travel democratized geographical mobility. National postal networks, enabled by rail, created the first mass information infrastructure. National newspaper distribution became possible. Perhaps most consequentially, railways integrated regional markets into national markets, enabling standardized manufactured goods to reach consumers far from production centers. This integration of market geography is the deployment-phase mechanism: the infrastructure of frenzy (the rails themselves) becomes the platform for a new consumer economy.

**Third Revolution deployment (c.1900s–1920s)**: The deployment of electrical infrastructure — itself built during the frenzy of the 1880s–1890s — transformed the domestic sphere. Household electrification in cities (first for lighting, then for appliances) began in the early 1900s but accelerated sharply after the post-WWI stabilization. The domestic appliance industry — electric irons, vacuum cleaners, refrigerators — emerged as a major consumer product category. The telephone spread from business to household use. The radio (technically a product of this revolution's synergy phase) became the first mass broadcast consumer medium by the 1920s. Department stores and mail-order retail (Sears, Roebuck) served the national consumer market enabled by rail and electrical infrastructure.

**Fourth Revolution deployment (c.1945–1973, the "Golden Age of Capitalism")**: The post-WWII consumer boom is the paradigmatic deployment golden age in Perez's framework. The turning point was the Great Depression and World War II — a brutal and extended crisis, but one that generated massive institutional reconstruction (New Deal, Bretton Woods, Marshall Plan, welfare states across Europe, GI Bill in the US) precisely calibrated to enable broad-based consumer market expansion. The result was the most rapid and inclusive rise in consumer living standards in recorded history across Western economies.

The consumer product categories that defined this golden age were precisely those that depended on the infrastructure built during the installation frenzy of the 1920s: automobile ownership became mass (from ~26% of American households in 1930 to ~79% by 1960), enabled by the road and highway infrastructure (culminating in the 1956 Interstate Highway Act) and cheap oil. Suburban homeownership expanded dramatically (US homeownership rose from ~44% in 1940 to ~62% by 1960). The domestic interior was transformed by mass-market refrigerators, washing machines, and dishwashers (Italian household refrigerator ownership rose from below 3% in the 1940s to over 94% by the early 1970s). Television became ubiquitous. Fast food, supermarkets, and mass tourism emerged as major industries. All of these were deployment-phase products: they required the installed base of automobiles, roads, electrical grids, and mass production know-how from the preceding installation period, but became accessible and widespread only under the post-war institutional conditions that enabled rising mass wages and consumer credit.

**Fifth Revolution deployment (c.2003–present)**: The ICT deployment golden age has produced a cascade of consumer product categories layered atop the internet and mobile infrastructure built during the dot-com frenzy: social media platforms (Facebook 2004, Twitter 2006, Instagram 2010), streaming services (Netflix streaming 2007, Spotify 2008), the smartphone economy (iPhone 2007, App Store 2008), platform-based services (Uber 2009, Airbnb 2008), e-commerce at mass scale (Amazon's explosive post-2005 growth), and cloud-based productivity tools (Google Docs, Slack, Zoom). Each of these products is structurally analogous to the post-war consumer goods: they required the installed infrastructure of high-bandwidth internet (built during dot-com frenzy) and affordable smartphones (themselves a product of the mobile bubble) to become mass-market products.

#### Product Category Patterns Per Phase

Across all five revolutions, a clear pattern emerges in product category dynamics:

*Irruption phase products*: Expensive, rough-edged products accessible only to wealthy early adopters or commercial users. High failure rate. More significant as demonstrations of what will be possible than as consumer products per se. (Early personal computers; first-generation mobile phones; initial internet applications.)

*Frenzy phase products*: Rapid proliferation of infrastructure and speculative ventures. Many consumer-facing products launched, but most financially unsustainable. Focus on market capture rather than profitability. The dot-com era produced thousands of e-commerce ventures, most of which failed; the infrastructure they built (fiber networks, fulfillment centers) survived and enabled the deployment phase.

*Synergy/deployment phase products*: Products that solve real, persistent problems for large populations, building on infrastructure now cheaply available. Characterized by business model sustainability, high penetration, and network effects. These are the products with the longest market lifespans — the automobile, the television, the smartphone, and streaming services all became multi-decade mass-market categories.

*Maturity phase products*: Incremental iterations on established categories; fierce price competition; consolidation into oligopolies. Innovation shifts to ancillary services and cost-reduction rather than category creation.

#### Strengths and Limitations of the Framework

**Strengths**: The Perez framework provides a historically grounded, macro-level account of why technological revolutions follow the pattern they do. It explains the recurrence of financial bubbles in revolutionary sectors without reducing them to irrational exuberance alone — they are structurally necessary for infrastructure buildout. It explains why crashes are not simply disasters but often preconditions for the productive deployment of revolutionary technologies. It provides a coherent account of the institutional dimension of economic development, treating the turning point not as an economic event alone but as a political-economic one. It has been validated across five distinct revolutions spanning different national contexts and institutional environments.

**Limitations**: The framework has been criticized on several grounds. First, *retrospective clarity*: the phases look clean in hindsight but are extremely difficult to identify in real-time. Perez herself acknowledged uncertainty about whether the 2001 and 2008 crashes together constituted the ICT revolution's full turning point or whether a third event was still pending. Second, *determinism*: critics argue the framework imposes a deterministic narrative onto historical events that were substantially more contingent. The specific character of each golden age depended on political choices, not just technological dynamics — the New Deal was not mechanically caused by the 1929 crash; it was a specific political response to it. Third, *timing*: the duration of each phase is highly variable. The fourth revolution's turning point lasted from 1929 to 1945 — sixteen years. The fifth revolution may have a double or triple turning point structure. This variability limits the framework's predictive precision. Fourth, *the overlap problem*: the emergence of AI, biotechnology, and green energy as potential new technological clusters while the ICT revolution is still in deployment creates an analytical challenge the original framework did not anticipate — the possibility of simultaneously being in the deployment phase of one revolution and the irruption phase of another.

### 4.2 The Installation vs. Deployment Phase Distinction

#### Theory and Mechanism

The installation/deployment distinction is perhaps the most practically important element of the Perez framework for product strategists. The two periods are not merely sequential but involve fundamentally different economic logics, capital dynamics, competitive landscapes, and opportunity structures.

During installation, the dominant logic is *exploration*: which technologies, infrastructure configurations, and business models will survive? Financial capital, oriented toward option value and high-variance returns, dominates. The appropriate strategy is aggressive experimentation, rapid iteration, and willingness to operate at a loss in exchange for market position. Infrastructure companies — those building the rails, networks, and platforms — are the primary value-capture entities.

During deployment, the dominant logic is *exploitation*: how can the now-cheaply-available infrastructure be most effectively used to deliver value to the broadest possible market? Production capital, oriented toward operational excellence, margin improvement, and market penetration, dominates. The appropriate strategy shifts toward product-market fit, sustainable unit economics, and deep integration with the installed infrastructure base.

As Jerry Neumann's analysis on Reaction Wheel identified, the deployment age brings several characteristic transformations: technology becomes invisible (features become expectations); innovation becomes smaller and more continuous; full-stack integration becomes the standard (not novel technology but excellent end-to-end execution); and customer development and iterative building — relatively unusual during installation's land-grab dynamics — become standard practice. The critical prediction is that in the deployment age, "there won't be online stores, there will just be stores" — the technology becomes the substrate, not the product.

The capital availability implication is significant: venture capital, structured around the high-variance logic of installation, becomes less suited to the incremental, integration-focused logic of deployment. The most successful deployment-phase companies — think McDonald's, Walmart, Ford's mass-market vehicles — often appear unimpressive by installation-phase metrics. They are not building new technology; they are achieving excellence at applying existing technology at scale and at accessible price points.

#### Historical Evidence

The post-WWII automobile deployment phase illustrates the pattern clearly. The automobile had been invented and installed (roads, refineries, dealerships, assembly lines) in the preceding installation period. The deployment phase did not produce new automobile *technology* so much as automobile *accessibility* — affordable mass-market models, installment purchasing plans, standardized service infrastructure, and the supporting ecosystem of fast food drive-ins, motels, and suburban retail that the car made possible. Walmart's emergence as America's largest company as a second-order consequence of automobile deployment (highways enabled suburbs; suburbs required big-box retail; big-box retail enabled Walmart's supply chain model) exemplifies the deployment-phase dynamic: the most successful deployment products often appear to be in a different industry than the revolutionary technology itself.

Similarly, the deployment phase of the second revolution — railways — produced the consumer innovation of the mass-market department store (Marshall Field, Harrods, Bon Marché). These were not railway companies, but they were only possible because railway-enabled national markets created the logistical scale for department store operations. The deployment-phase opportunity was not in the infrastructure itself but in what could be built on top of it.

### 4.3 Financial Capital vs. Production Capital Dynamics

#### Theory and Mechanism

Financial capital, in Perez's account, is not inherently dysfunctional — it is a necessary lubricant for the early-stage development of technological revolutions that no rational production-capital enterprise would fund. The expected returns from truly revolutionary technology are too uncertain, the time horizons too long, and the required scale of infrastructure investment too large for production-oriented capital to finance unaided. Financial capital's willingness to fund high-risk, high-upside ventures during irruption and frenzy is precisely what gets the infrastructure built.

The dysfunction emerges in the frenzy phase, when financial capital, having found a highly profitable new domain, begins generating returns from financial engineering rather than productive investment. The mechanism: as the revolutionary sector becomes demonstrably profitable, more capital floods in, driving up asset prices beyond what fundamentals support. Financial intermediaries, competing for returns, innovate in financial instruments — new forms of debt, derivatives, structures — that amplify returns but also amplify instability. The gap between asset prices and productive value widens until the crash forces reconciliation.

Perez's description of the frenzy dynamic is that financial capital becomes "inebriated" — not irrational exactly, but operating within a self-referential logic where paper gains justify further paper gains, disconnected from whether underlying enterprises are creating real value. This is the mechanism behind the dot-com bubble: companies with no revenue, no plausible path to profitability, and often no differentiated technology were capitalized at billions because the market had shifted from valuing productive capacity to valuing the signal of participation in the new technological wave.

The post-crash return to productive logic is forced, not chosen: the evaporation of paper gains humbles financial capital and creates political conditions for regulatory reconstruction. The institutions that emerge from this reconstruction — whether the New Deal financial regulations of the 1930s, or the SEC regulations and corporate governance reforms after various market crashes — create the conditions under which financial capital is channeled toward productive deployment rather than speculative excess.

#### Hype Cycles, Speculation, Correction, and Real Value Creation

The Gartner Hype Cycle, while operating at a much shorter time scale and narrower analytical level than the Perez framework, captures a related dynamic at the product level: the "peak of inflated expectations" followed by the "trough of disillusionment" and eventual "plateau of productivity" maps loosely onto the frenzy/crash/deployment sequence. The important difference is that Perez's account is explicitly macro-economic and financial, not just perceptual — the crash is not merely a correction of inflated expectations but an actual destruction of financial capital and an actual realignment of institutional incentives.

For consumer product strategists, the financial capital/production capital dynamic has a direct implication: products built and scaled during frenzy phases tend to require sustained external capital (venture funding, public market enthusiasm) to stay operational, because their unit economics do not yet support profitability. When the capital tap turns off at the turning point, products dependent on financial-capital subsidies face existential pressure, while products that have achieved genuine production-capital logic (real margins, sustainable unit economics, genuine consumer value) can survive and dominate the deployment phase. The 2022–2023 "funding winter" for consumer tech is interpretable in exactly these terms: it forced a correction from financial-capital logic (growth-at-any-cost) to production-capital logic (path to profitability).

#### Historical Frenzy/Crash Sequences

*Railway mania (1840s)* → *crash* → Cheap, widely available railway infrastructure becomes the platform for the Victorian deployment golden age.

*Gilded Age electrical speculation (1880s–1890s)* → *Panic of 1907* → Cheap electrical infrastructure becomes the platform for mass appliance consumption in the 1910s–1920s.

*Roaring Twenties speculation (1920s)* → *1929 Crash/Great Depression* → New Deal institutional reconstruction enables post-WWII deployment golden age.

*Dot-com frenzy (1995–2000)* → *Crash 2000–2002* → Cheap fiber and internet infrastructure becomes the platform for the Web 2.0/smartphone/app economy deployment phase.

*SaaS/Unicorn frenzy (2015–2021)* → *2022 funding winter* → Potential transition to disciplined deployment-phase B2C product development.

*AI frenzy (2023–present?)* → *crash TBD* → AI infrastructure becomes the platform for an AI-deployment consumer product wave?

### 4.4 The Current ICT/Digital Revolution: Where Are We?

#### Theory and Mechanism

Determining current positioning in the Perez cycle is the central practical challenge for anyone applying the framework. The difficulty is structural: the four phases are identifiable with clarity only in retrospect, using data that is not available in real-time. In real-time, participants are operating in a present that looks simultaneously like the late stages of the previous phase and the early stages of the next.

Perez's own view as of 2024 is that the ICT revolution has experienced a *double turning point* — the 2000–2002 dot-com crash and the 2008 financial crisis — and that we are now in the early-to-mid synergy phase of the ICT deployment period. However, she places this with a critical qualification: the golden age has not yet fully materialized because the institutional conditions for it (progressive taxation, digital infrastructure investment, green industrial policy, global demand creation) have not been adequately constructed. In her terminology, western economies are at or past the turning point but the synergy phase's full productive potential has been impeded by insufficient institutional reform.

AI presents a further complication. Perez's 2024 article in Project Syndicate ("What Is AI's Place in History?") argues that AI is not an independent new technological revolution but rather "a key development within the still-evolving information-communications-technology (ICT) revolution, which started in the 1970s with the microprocessor." On this reading, AI is an acceleration of the ICT revolution's deployment phase — the application layer being built atop the installed base of internet, cloud, and mobile infrastructure — not a new irruption in the Perez sense.

An alternative reading, advanced by several analysts, holds that AI (specifically the large language model revolution triggered by the transformer architecture of 2017 and ChatGPT's 2022 launch) constitutes a new irruption event, with its own frenzy, turning point, and deployment trajectory. On this reading, we are simultaneously in the deployment phase of the fifth (ICT) revolution and the early irruption/frenzy phase of a nascent sixth revolution centered on AI/biotech/green energy.

#### Current Positioning: Multiple Analytical Views

**View 1 — Mid-Deployment of ICT (Perez's own position, c.2024)**: The ICT revolution's double turning point (2000 + 2008) is complete. We are in the synergy phase, with broad technology diffusion underway but institutional conditions insufficiently configured for an inclusive golden age. AI is an ICT deployment phase product. Policy directive is needed to prevent the deployment benefits concentrating in a narrow tech oligopoly.

**View 2 — Late Installation / Transitional (Perez c.2010–2015, applied forward)**: We are at the turning point or just past it. The AI/crypto boom of 2020–2021 represents a continuation of ICT installation frenzy dynamics. The 2022 funding winter and crypto crash may be completing the turning point. The full deployment golden age remains ahead.

**View 3 — New Revolution Irruption (various analysts, 2023–2025)**: The AI revolution (triggered by transformers and LLMs) constitutes a new technological revolution in its own right, currently in the irruption/early frenzy phase. Massive infrastructure investment (GPU clusters, data centers, foundation model training) mirrors the railway and fiber buildout of prior installation periods. A crash and turning point for AI specifically is ahead; the deployment golden age of AI is further in the future.

**View 4 — Overlapping Cycles**: We are simultaneously in the late deployment/maturity of the ICT revolution and the early installation of an AI/green/biotech cluster revolution. Overlapping cycles explain the apparent anomaly of continued speculative frenzy dynamics (AI valuations, crypto) alongside genuine deployment golden age characteristics (smartphone mass market, streaming, e-commerce at full penetration) in the same historical moment.

#### Evidence and Market Indicators

Several market-level observations are consistent with multiple views simultaneously:

- Global AI private investment reached $109.1 billion in the US alone in 2025, with exponential capital flows into GPU infrastructure — consistent with late frenzy or early irruption dynamics.
- The 2022–2023 funding winter for consumer tech (Perez-framework frenzy correction) produced a forced rationalization of unit economics — consistent with a turning point dynamic within the broader ICT cycle.
- Platform consolidation is accelerating: the dominance of Google, Apple, Meta, Amazon, and Microsoft is consistent with maturity-phase oligopolization of the ICT revolution's infrastructure layer.
- AI productivity gains remain contested: "companies are failing to make expected AI benefits work" and "business uptake of AI tools is stalling" (MIT Technology Review, 2025) — consistent with either early deployment disappointment (pre-golden age) or genuine frenzy excess that has not yet been productively deployed.
- The "great AI hype correction of 2025" (MIT Technology Review, December 2025) has begun to separate AI infrastructure investments (high and sustained) from AI application valuations (more volatile), which mirrors the dot-com-era separation of infrastructure (fiber, servers) from application layer valuations.

#### AI as New Installation Phase or Deployment Acceleration

The practical difference between the "AI as ICT deployment" and "AI as new irruption" interpretations matters substantially for product strategy:

Under the *deployment acceleration* interpretation: AI consumer products built now are deployment-phase products. They should follow deployment-phase logic — solve real problems, achieve sustainable unit economics, serve mainstream users, leverage cheaply available AI infrastructure (API costs have dropped by 99% since 2020). The appropriate strategic posture is disciplined product development, not speculative land-grab.

Under the *new installation* interpretation: AI consumer products built now are irruption/frenzy-phase products. The appropriate posture is aggressive market-capture, tolerance for losses, and positioning for infrastructure ownership. The deployment golden age of AI consumer products is years or decades away, after the crash, institutional reconstruction, and broad infrastructure maturation.

Perez's own framing suggests the former: AI is infrastructure-as-deployment, not a new installation wave. The implication is that the opportunity for AI-enabled consumer products is *now*, using the cheaply available AI infrastructure to deliver genuinely useful services at scale.

### 4.5 Applications to Consumer Product Strategy

#### Theory and Mechanism

The Perez framework provides a structural basis for reasoning about the types of consumer products that thrive in each phase. The key mechanism is the relationship between product opportunity and infrastructure availability: products that require infrastructure that does not yet exist cannot achieve mass-market penetration regardless of quality; products that leverage newly cheap infrastructure at scale define the deployment golden age.

This structural insight cuts against the dominant VC narrative, which privileges radical novelty and disruption at all points in the cycle. Radical novelty is appropriate during installation — when the question is which infrastructure will define the next paradigm. But during deployment, radical novelty is often a liability: the winning products are frequently the ones that most effectively apply already-installed infrastructure to previously unresolved problems for mainstream users, not the ones that push toward the next technological frontier.

The deployment golden age consumer product has a characteristic signature: it takes something previously available only to wealthy or commercial users and makes it accessible to the mass market, using infrastructure that became cheap after the installation crash. The automobile made personal mobility mass-market (previously the province of the wealthy). The washing machine made laundry automation mass-market. Netflix made video entertainment library access mass-market (previously requiring a video rental store trip). Uber made point-to-point transportation dispatch mass-market (previously requiring a phone call to a taxi dispatcher and waiting in uncertainty). In each case, the product was not technologically novel at the time of mass-market deployment — the technology had existed for years in more expensive or inconvenient form. The deployment was the innovation.

#### Products by Phase: ICT Revolution Specific Patterns

**ICT Installation Irruption (1971–mid-1990s)**:
Consumer-facing products were thin: early personal computers (Apple II, 1977; IBM PC, 1981), primarily for enthusiasts and commercial users. Consumer online services (AOL, CompuServe) for early adopters. The internet was largely pre-consumer. The primary value creation was in the business and infrastructure layers.

**ICT Installation Frenzy (mid-1990s–2000)**:
The first wave of consumer internet products: Amazon (1994), eBay (1995), Yahoo! (1995), Google (1998). Most were financially unsustainable at launch. Thousands of e-commerce, content, and portal companies launched and failed. The infrastructure of fiber networks, web server capacity, and payment processing was built during this period — often at loss — and survived the crash as cheaply available substrate for the next phase.

**ICT Deployment Synergy (2003–present)**:
The consumer product golden age: Facebook (2004), YouTube (2005), Twitter (2006), iPhone (2007), App Store (2008), Airbnb (2008), Spotify (2008), Uber (2009), Instagram (2010), Pinterest (2010), Snapchat (2011), WhatsApp (2009/2014 acquisition), Netflix streaming (2007). Each of these products: (a) built on infrastructure made cheap by the dot-com frenzy (broadband, smartphone hardware, app distribution platforms); (b) solved a real, previously unresolved problem for mainstream users; (c) achieved sustainable (or sustainable-in-principle) unit economics; (d) served mass-market rather than early-adopter populations. The smartphone deployment wave (2008–2018) particularly exemplifies the deployment dynamic: it was the platform that unlocked the app economy's consumer products, just as roads unlocked automobile-dependent consumer products.

**AI-Enabled Products (2022–present, deployment-acceleration frame)**:
Under the deployment-acceleration interpretation, the current AI-enabled consumer product wave has the character of a deployment-phase opportunity. The infrastructure (foundation models, cheap API inference, cloud compute) is installed and cheaply available. The opportunity is to build consumer products that leverage this infrastructure to solve real problems for mainstream users: AI-powered health monitoring and medical guidance, personalized education platforms, AI-assisted financial planning for ordinary consumers, cognitive accessibility tools for people with disabilities, AI-native productivity applications for non-technical users, and AI-enabled creative tools for non-professionals. These products fit the deployment pattern: they take capabilities previously available only to wealthy consumers (personal financial advisors, private tutors, medical specialists, creative professionals) and make them accessible at mass-market price points, using infrastructure made cheap by the AI frenzy.

#### What Types of Products Thrive in Each Phase

*Irruption phase consumer products*:
- Technically novel, expensive, rough-edged
- Appeal to enthusiasts and technically sophisticated early adopters
- Define new categories but reach small markets
- Infrastructure-dependent: require significant user sophistication to access
- Business model: uncertain, often venture-funded at loss
- Historical examples: early automobiles (1890s–1907), early personal computers (1977–1984), early smartphones (2000–2006), early AI assistants (2015–2022)

*Frenzy phase consumer products*:
- Rapid proliferation, many competing offerings
- Focus on market capture at expense of sustainability
- Often require infrastructure subsidies (below-cost offerings to win market share)
- Business model: growth-at-all-costs, venture-funded, often money-losing
- Historical examples: dot-com e-commerce (1998–2000), ride-sharing/food delivery blitzscaling (2012–2020), BNPL consumer finance (2019–2022)

*Synergy/deployment phase consumer products*:
- Solve real, persistent problems for mainstream users
- Build on newly cheap infrastructure
- Achieve sustainable unit economics
- Accessible price points; mass-market penetration
- Business model: profitable at scale, defensible through network effects or distribution
- Historical examples: post-war appliances and automobiles, web 2.0 platforms, mobile app economy

*Maturity phase consumer products*:
- Incremental feature improvement
- Price competition dominant
- Market consolidation; oligopoly dynamics
- Business model: margin-focused, efficient operations
- Historical examples: 1960s television market, 2020s social media platforms, mature e-commerce

#### The Deployment Phase Product Strategy Imperative

For consumer product developers operating in the current environment (2026), the deployment-phase logic suggests several structural patterns worth noting:

1. **Infrastructure is cheap; application is the opportunity.** AI inference costs, cloud compute, mobile distribution, and payment infrastructure are all at commodity prices. The installation work of the previous decades is complete and cheaply available. The opportunity is not to rebuild infrastructure but to apply it to unresolved consumer problems.

2. **Real problems over speculative hype.** Deployment-phase products historically differentiate from frenzy-phase products precisely by solving problems that existed before the technology — not problems created by the technology itself. The washing machine didn't create the problem of dirty clothes; it solved an ancient problem using new infrastructure. Deployment-phase AI consumer products should similarly identify genuine, pre-existing problems and use AI to reduce friction or cost, not create new categories of anxiety or engagement-maximization.

3. **Unit economics matter again.** The post-2022 funding environment reflects the shift from financial-capital logic to production-capital logic: investors are back to demanding paths to profitability. This is a structural deployment-phase characteristic, not a temporary market correction.

4. **Mainstream users, not early adopters.** Golden age consumer products reach populations well beyond enthusiasts. The deployment phase is when the benefits of the technological revolution are distributed broadly. Products optimized for early adopters miss the primary deployment-phase opportunity.

5. **Second-order effects are large.** The Walmart effect — the emergence of entirely new consumer categories as second-order consequences of the deployed infrastructure — historically produces some of the most valuable deployment-phase companies. Just as Walmart required highways (not computers), some of the most valuable AI-era consumer companies may be in domains (healthcare access, education, housing, mobility) not obviously adjacent to AI itself, but enabled by AI infrastructure lowering costs in those domains.

## 5. Comparative Synthesis

### 5.1 Cross-Cutting Trade-off Table

The Perez framework sits alongside several other timing and diffusion frameworks that consumer product strategists commonly employ. The following table positions them comparatively:

| Framework | Time Horizon | Unit of Analysis | Core Mechanism | Predictive Focus | Key Limitation |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Perez Technological Revolutions** | 50–70 years per revolution | Entire economy / paradigm shift | Financial capital / production capital cycle; installation vs. deployment | When to build infrastructure vs. applications; golden age timing | Phase identification in real-time extremely difficult; limited predictive precision |
| **Rogers Diffusion of Innovations** | 10–30 years per innovation | Individual technology adoption | Social learning; opinion leadership; adopter categories (innovators → laggards) | When a specific technology will reach mainstream | Does not account for financial capital dynamics or inter-revolutionary transitions |
| **Moore's Crossing the Chasm** | 3–10 years | Product lifecycle within one technology | The chasm between early adopter and early majority (pragmatist) market | How to cross from niche to mainstream for a specific product | Tactical, not strategic; no account of macro-economic cycle context |
| **S-Curve / Technology Life Cycle** | Variable | Technology performance trajectory | Diminishing returns to R&D; physical limits of performance improvement | When to switch from one technology to the next | Describes technology performance, not economic deployment or financial dynamics |
| **Gartner Hype Cycle** | 3–10 years | Technology expectations vs. reality | Peak of inflated expectations; trough of disillusionment; plateau of productivity | Manages expectations about specific technologies | No financial capital mechanism; no account of paradigm shifts or long-wave dynamics |
| **Clayton Christensen Disruptive Innovation** | Variable | Firm and market dynamics | Low-end or new-market entry disrupting incumbents | When incumbents will be displaced by new entrants | Focused on firm-level dynamics; does not explain why revolutions cluster temporally |

### 5.2 Key Distinctions

The Perez framework is unique among these in operating at the macro-economic, long-wave level. It is not a theory of how individual firms succeed or fail, or how specific products cross from early adopter to mainstream. It is a theory of why technological revolutions follow cyclical patterns of financial frenzy, institutional crisis, and productive deployment — and why the timing of these macro-cycles matters for the types of products and strategies that are appropriate.

The Rogers framework is complementary but operates at a different scale: it describes the social diffusion of an already-established innovation, without accounting for the financial capital dynamics that determine whether the innovation gets funded and infrastructure gets built in the first place. The two frameworks can be nested: within the deployment phase of a Perez revolution, Rogers-style diffusion curves describe how specific applications spread through the population.

Moore's chasm framework is tactical relative to Perez's strategic. Crossing the chasm from early adopter to early majority is a challenge that occurs repeatedly throughout both installation and deployment phases, at the product level. But the *probability* of crossing the chasm successfully depends heavily on which phase of the Perez cycle the broader paradigm is in: in the synergy phase, when infrastructure is cheap and institutions are aligned, the chasm is structurally easier to cross for applications built on the installed base.

The S-curve literature (Foster, 1986; Utterback, 1994) tracks the performance trajectory of specific technologies and their eventual substitution by superior alternatives. This overlaps with the maturity/irruption transition in Perez's framework — the point at which one paradigm's S-curve flattens is often when the next irruption begins — but S-curve analysis operates at the technology level without Perez's financial capital mechanism.

### 5.3 Synthesis: When to Use Which Framework

For *macro-strategic timing* (should we be building infrastructure or applications? is this a frenzy or a golden age environment?): Perez is most relevant.

For *product-level adoption strategy* (how do we reach the mainstream? what are the adopter segments?): Rogers and Moore are most relevant.

For *technology investment decisions* (when is this technology approaching its limits? what's the next S-curve?): S-curve analysis is most relevant.

For *competitive strategy against incumbents* (how do we use disruption to enter markets?): Christensen is most relevant.

For *expectation management about specific AI/emerging tech investments*: Gartner Hype Cycle is most relevant at short time horizons.

## 6. Open Problems and Gaps

### 6.1 The Phase Identification Problem

The most persistent practical limitation of the Perez framework is the difficulty of identifying current phase in real-time. The framework provides excellent retrospective explanatory power: looking back at the dot-com frenzy, the 2000 crash, the post-2008 deployment of the app economy, the phases are clearly legible. Looking forward from any present moment, they are not. Perez herself has revised her phase assessments over time — in the early 2000s positioning western economies at the turning point; in the 2010s arguing the turning point was complete but the golden age required policy action; in the 2020s characterizing AI as a deployment-phase development rather than a new installation irruption.

This is not a criticism unique to Perez — all long-wave frameworks face the same problem. But it limits the framework's use as a decision tool. A practitioner who concluded in 2016 that "we are in the deployment golden age of ICT" would have been partially right (the app economy golden age was in full swing) but would have missed the subsequent AI frenzy dynamics of 2020–2023. The appropriate use of the framework is not as a precise positioning instrument but as a set of structural questions: where is capital flowing? is infrastructure being built or applications being deployed? are unit economics improving or deteriorating? are markets expanding to new populations or consolidating among existing adopters?

### 6.2 AI as Paradigm Within Paradigm

The status of AI within the Perez framework remains genuinely contested and analytically unresolved. The two dominant positions — AI as ICT deployment acceleration (Perez's view) versus AI as new technological revolution (various analysts) — have different empirical predictions that are beginning to become distinguishable.

If AI is a deployment acceleration: we would expect AI applications to follow deployment-phase logic relatively quickly — rapid cost reduction, mainstream user adoption, sustainable business models, and broad economic diffusion. The AI productivity dividend should show up in macroeconomic data within a short horizon (years rather than decades).

If AI is a new installation irruption: we would expect continued speculative excess, followed eventually by a crash and institutional reconstruction before the AI deployment golden age. The productivity dividend would arrive only after the institutional conditions for it are established, potentially decades out.

Evidence from 2025 is mixed: AI infrastructure investment continues at frenzy levels, but application-layer adoption is stalling in some domains (MIT Technology Review, 2025). AI costs are dropping at rates consistent with deployment-phase dynamics, but enterprise adoption patterns show the "peak of inflated expectations" correction characteristic of pre-mainstream hype. The most honest assessment is that both dynamics are present simultaneously, consistent with the "overlapping cycles" hypothesis.

### 6.3 The Institutional Gap Critique

Perez's framework consistently emphasizes that the quality of the deployment golden age depends on institutional reconstruction at the turning point. The ICT revolution's 2000 and 2008 turning points produced relatively weak institutional reform compared to the post-1929 reconstruction. The regulatory response to the dot-com crash was primarily technocratic (Sarbanes-Oxley corporate governance reform); the 2008 response was primarily financial stabilization without fundamental redistribution of economic power. Neither generated the kind of New Deal-scale institutional reconstruction that characterized the fourth revolution's turning point.

The implication in Perez's framework is that the ICT golden age is stunted relative to its potential — the technology is deployed, but its benefits are distributed too narrowly. The concentration of value in a handful of platform oligopolies (Google, Apple, Meta, Amazon, Microsoft) is exactly the maturity-phase oligopolization that the framework predicts, but occurring earlier and more completely than historical golden ages, precisely because the institutional conditions for broader distribution were not established.

This is an empirically testable prediction: if the Perez framework is correct, the ICT revolution's deployment phase should, in the absence of additional institutional reform, produce a narrower golden age than the fourth revolution — real productivity gains, but distributed disproportionately to capital owners and technology workers, with limited mass-market wage gains. Available data from 2000–2025 is at least roughly consistent with this prediction.

### 6.4 Geographic Heterogeneity

The Perez framework was developed primarily from the perspective of leading economies (first Britain, then the US and Germany, then the US more generally). The framework acknowledges geographic heterogeneity in "catching-up" dynamics — late-developing economies can leapfrog installation phases by adopting already-deployed paradigm technology — but the consumer product implications are understated. Some of the most significant deployment-phase consumer product opportunities in the current ICT revolution are in emerging markets precisely because they lack the institutional lock-in to prior paradigm infrastructure. Africa's mobile money revolution (M-Pesa), India's digital payments infrastructure (UPI), and China's super-app ecosystem all represent deployment-phase innovations that built on cheaply available ICT infrastructure to serve populations that the prior paradigm had excluded.

### 6.5 Environmental and Sustainability Constraints

The Perez framework was developed largely without attention to environmental limits. The post-WWII golden age consumer boom — the paradigmatic deployment success — generated environmental costs (automobile dependence, suburban sprawl, fossil fuel consumption) that have become major constraints on subsequent development. Perez's more recent work has addressed this explicitly, arguing that the next golden age must be "smart, green, fair and global" — that sustainability constraints are not merely external limits on the deployment dynamic but should be positive drivers of it, through "tilting the playing field" toward green technologies. This evolution of the framework is intellectually important but remains theoretically underdeveloped relative to the core installation/deployment analysis.

### 6.6 Predictive Failures and Anomalies

The framework predicts that the turning point crisis will generate institutional reform sufficient to enable a broad golden age. This prediction has been most clearly validated for the fourth revolution (post-1929 Depression / post-WWII) and least clearly validated for the fifth (post-2000 / post-2008). The fifth revolution's turning point may simply have been inadequate — generating stabilization without transformation — in which case the predicted golden age has not been aborted but merely delayed, pending more adequate institutional reconstruction.

Alternatively, structural features of the digital economy (zero-marginal-cost production, platform network effects, data asymmetries) may generate monopoly dynamics that the institutional toolkit developed for the manufacturing economy cannot adequately address. This would represent a genuine challenge to the framework's universality: the same underlying dynamic (installation → crash → deployment) but with a different institutional resolution trajectory than the historical pattern would predict.

## 7. Conclusion

The Carlota Perez framework of techno-economic paradigm shifts is one of the most powerful macro-level conceptual tools available for understanding the long-wave dynamics of technological change and its relationship to financial markets, institutional evolution, and consumer product opportunity. Across five technological revolutions spanning 250 years, the framework identifies a consistent two-period structure — installation and deployment — separated by a financial crash and institutional turning point, generating the characteristic golden age of mass-market consumer product adoption in the deployment period.

For consumer product strategists in 2026, the framework's most actionable insight is structural: deployment-phase products solve real, persistent problems for mainstream users, using infrastructure that became cheaply available after the installation crash, and they achieve sustainable unit economics rather than relying on financial-capital subsidies. They are not the most technically novel products; they are the products that most effectively democratize access to capabilities previously confined to expensive, inconvenient, or expert-only channels.

The current positioning of the ICT/digital revolution remains genuinely contested. Perez's own analysis places us in the early-to-mid synergy phase of ICT deployment, with AI as a deployment-phase accelerant rather than a new installation irruption. Alternative analyses hold that AI constitutes a new installation frenzy, with the deployment golden age of AI consumer products still ahead of a coming crash. The most intellectually honest position acknowledges the evidence for both interpretations and treats the framework as a set of structural diagnostic questions rather than a precise timing instrument.

What is not contested is the historical pattern itself: every previous technological revolution has produced, in its deployment phase, a wave of consumer products that achieved mass-market penetration by applying newly cheap infrastructure to genuine human needs. That wave is the most durable source of long-run economic value creation in the Perez framework. Whether it is currently underway, nearly here, or still some years away depends on institutional and political choices — about regulation, distribution, and the direction of technological investment — that the framework identifies as necessary but does not mechanically predict.

The open problems are significant: phase identification in real-time remains hard; the AI-as-revolution question is unresolved; institutional reform following the ICT turning point has been weaker than the fourth revolution pattern would predict; and environmental constraints create a new set of requirements for what a "golden age" can mean. These gaps do not invalidate the framework — they define the frontier of its productive development.

### Diagnostic Questions for Current Positioning

The following questions, derived from the Perez framework, can be used diagnostically to assess current positioning for any technology or market:

1. Is primary value flowing to infrastructure builders or application developers? (Infrastructure = installation; application = deployment)
2. Are leading companies profitable at their current scale or dependent on ongoing capital raises? (Profitable = deployment; capital-dependent = installation/frenzy)
3. Is the technology invisible or novel? (Invisible = deployment; novel = installation)
4. Are product-market conversations about "what problem does this solve?" or "what new category does this create?" (Problem-solving = deployment; category-creation = installation)
5. Are returns driven by production improvements or financial engineering? (Production = deployment; financial = frenzy)
6. Is the technology reaching new (lower-income, less technical, geographically broader) user populations? (Broad penetration = deployment golden age)
