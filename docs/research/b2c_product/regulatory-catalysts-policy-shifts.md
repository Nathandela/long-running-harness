# Regulatory Catalysts & Policy Shifts as Market Creators

*2026-03-18*

---

## Abstract

Regulation is conventionally framed as a constraint on markets — a friction imposed by the state that raises compliance costs, restricts entry, and slows innovation. This framing, while applicable in some contexts, misses a more consequential dynamic: regulation frequently acts as the generative mechanism that brings entire markets into existence. Whether through mandated data portability that forces incumbents to share infrastructure, compliance complexity that rewards sophisticated early movers, prohibition reversals that unlock suppressed demand, or sustainability requirements that create product categories overnight, the state shapes the opportunity landscape as much as any technology.

This paper surveys the mechanisms by which regulatory change creates markets rather than merely constraining them. Drawing on economics theory — from Stigler's capture model to public interest frameworks to more recent work on market-creating versus market-constraining regulation — and supported by empirical data across six major regulatory domains (data privacy, open banking, regulatory unlocks in telehealth and controlled substances, right-to-repair, AI regulation, and ESG/carbon markets), we develop a taxonomy of regulatory market-creation patterns and analyze their structural characteristics, including timing windows, moat durability, and reversal risk.

The central finding is that regulatory market creation is neither accidental nor uniform. Different regulatory mechanisms produce structurally distinct market opportunities: compliance moats generate durable incumbency advantages; mandated portability generates transient first-mover windows that quickly commoditize; prohibition reversals generate time-compressed gold rushes with high winner-take-most dynamics; and sustainability mandates generate diffuse, long-duration demand creation across multiple product categories. Understanding which mechanism is at play governs how one should position — and how long any competitive advantage created by regulation is likely to last.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Problem Statement

The question of whether regulation helps or hinders innovation has generated decades of academic debate. The conventional economic critique, dating from Stigler (1971) and formalized in Chicago School thinking, holds that regulation imposes deadweight loss, restricts entry, and primarily serves incumbent interests. The public interest counterargument holds that regulation corrects genuine market failures — externalities, information asymmetries, natural monopolies — and that well-designed regulation can improve welfare.

Both traditions, however, share an implicit frame: they treat regulation as a modification to an existing market. Less attention has been paid to cases where regulation does not modify a market but instead calls a market into being. When the EU's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) came into effect in May 2018, it did not merely impose fines on data handlers — it created an addressable market for privacy software that had scarcely existed before, currently valued at approximately $6.84 billion and growing at a 15% CAGR through 2030. When U.S. states began legalizing cannabis between 2012 and 2024, they did not merely regulate an existing legal market — they transferred approximately $30 billion in annual consumption from an unregulated shadow economy into a fully formed industry employing over 425,000 people. When PSD2 mandated that European banks expose customer data via standardized APIs, it did not merely regulate banking — it bootstrapped an open banking infrastructure that now underpins a $31 billion global fintech market growing at 27% annually.

These are not edge cases. They represent a recurring and undertheorized pattern: the state as market architect.

### 1.2 Scope and Key Definitions

This paper examines **regulatory market creation** — situations where a government policy intervention is the proximate cause of a new product or service market materializing, expanding dramatically, or being restructured in ways that create durable commercial opportunity. We focus on six domains with the strongest empirical support and clearest market creation dynamics: data privacy regulation, open banking and data portability, regulatory unlocks (telehealth, cannabis, psychedelics, crypto), right-to-repair, AI governance, and ESG/carbon mandates.

We define the following terms as used throughout:

- **Regulatory catalyst**: A specific piece of legislation, rulemaking, or enforcement action that triggers identifiable market creation or expansion.
- **Compliance moat**: A competitive advantage derived from the cost or complexity of achieving regulatory compliance, such that early or sophisticated compliers deter later entrants.
- **Prohibition reversal**: A regulatory shift from prohibition or severe restriction to legal permissibility, releasing suppressed latent demand.
- **Mandated portability**: A regulatory requirement that incumbents expose data, infrastructure, or capabilities to third parties via standardized interfaces.
- **Timing window**: The period during which first-mover advantage from a regulatory change is defensible before commoditization.

## 2. Foundations: Regulatory Economics Theory

### 2.1 The Public Interest Tradition

The earliest systematic economics of regulation treated it as an instrument of market failure correction. Pigou (1920) established the theoretical basis for corrective taxation of negative externalities. Subsequent public interest theorists framed regulation as a welfare-improving response to natural monopoly, information asymmetry, and coordination problems. On this view, the market creation effect of regulation is incidental: the goal is to correct failure, and any new markets that emerge are byproducts of a remediation exercise.

### 2.2 Stigler and the Capture Model

George Stigler's 1971 paper "The Theory of Economic Regulation" fundamentally challenged the public interest frame. Stigler argued that regulation is not merely a correction mechanism but a product traded in a political marketplace. Industries, he contended, actively seek regulation that serves their interests — particularly barriers to entry that protect incumbents from competition. The regulatory agency, over time, is "captured" by the industry it regulates.

Stigler's model carries a direct implication for market creation: when incumbents seek and obtain protective regulation, they are not only defending existing positions but often creating structural advantages that effectively build moats against new entrants. The GDPR compliance moat dynamic discussed in Section 4.1 is a contemporary illustration — large technology companies with existing compliance infrastructure benefit disproportionately from rules that impose high fixed compliance costs on smaller entrants.

### 2.3 Market-Creating Versus Market-Constraining Regulation

More recent empirical work has attempted to decompose regulation's heterogeneous effects on innovation and market development. The OECD's Product Market Regulation indicators, developed across nearly 50 years of cross-country data, find that deregulation in network industries raised labor productivity by approximately 5% on average, with manufacturing sectors experiencing double the gains due to upstream dependencies. But this finding captures the productivity effects of *removing* restrictions; it says less about how *adding* regulation in specific forms can create markets.

A 2022 study in *Organization Science* distinguishes between "protective regulation" — which creates predictability and stable expectations for investment — and "restrictive regulation" — which prescribes negative behaviors and reduces the option space for firms. Protective regulation, the study finds, has a positive effect on innovation because it reduces uncertainty for investors. Restrictive regulation, by contrast, tends to reduce innovation by limiting the adjacent possible. This distinction maps closely to the taxonomy developed in Section 3.

### 2.4 Regulatory Arbitrage as Market Signal

Regulatory arbitrage — the exploitation of inconsistencies across regulatory regimes — is typically treated as a pathology, a form of avoidance that thwarts regulatory intent. But arbitrage also functions as a price signal in the market for regulatory products. When firms consistently migrate toward lighter regulatory environments, or when entire industries organize around exploiting gaps between regulatory categories, this reveals where regulation is generating rents that the market is attempting to capture. The early history of the gig economy — companies like Uber explicitly constructing their labor model around independent contractor classification to avoid employment regulation — illustrates how regulatory gap-seeking can itself drive product architecture and market formation.

### 2.5 The Sandboxing Phenomenon and Market Seeding

A relatively recent innovation in regulatory design — the regulatory sandbox — explicitly uses regulation as a market incubation tool. Introduced by the UK's Financial Conduct Authority in 2016 and since adopted by over 50 jurisdictions, sandboxes allow firms to test regulated products and services with reduced regulatory burden under controlled conditions. Deloitte's analysis of the UK fintech sandbox found that firms selected for participation witnessed up to a 15% increase in capital raised, demonstrating that regulatory participation itself becomes a quality signal attracting private capital. Singapore's autonomous vehicle sandbox attracted foreign investment and positioned the country among top nations for AV adoption. This is regulation as an active market-seeding mechanism.

## 3. Taxonomy of Regulatory Market-Creation Mechanisms

The following taxonomy classifies the primary mechanisms by which regulation creates markets, based on the domains analyzed in this paper. Each mechanism has distinct structural properties that govern the type of opportunity created, the durability of competitive advantage, and the timing characteristics that determine when to enter.

| Mechanism | Definition | Primary Driver | Timing Character | Moat Type | Reversal Risk |
|-----------|-----------|----------------|------------------|-----------|---------------|
| **Compliance Moat** | Regulation imposes compliance costs that benefit early/sophisticated movers as barriers to entry | Complexity + fixed cost | Slow build, durable | Operational scale + certifications | Low (regulation rarely repealed once entrenched) |
| **Mandated Portability** | Incumbents required to expose data/infrastructure to third parties | Data access mandate | Fast creation, rapid commoditization | First-mover distribution + network effects | Medium (political will may shift) |
| **Prohibition Reversal** | Legal permissibility replaces prohibition, releasing suppressed demand | Political/social unlock | Time-compressed gold rush | Licensing, geography, brand trust | High (political backlash possible) |
| **Standards Creation** | Regulation establishes interoperability standards, enabling an ecosystem | Coordination problem solution | Network-effect driven | Platform position in ecosystem | Low-medium |
| **Consumer Empowerment** | Regulation gives consumers rights that create new service demand | Information asymmetry correction | Gradual adoption curve | Service quality, certification | Low |
| **ESG/Mandate Cascade** | Sustainability requirements propagate through supply chains, creating product demand | Compliance obligation | Long duration, diffuse | Category creation + reporting infrastructure | Medium-High (political cycles) |

## 4. Analysis

### 4.1 Regulation as Compliance Moat: The GDPR Model

#### Theory and Mechanism

The GDPR, which entered into force on May 25, 2018, is the most extensively documented case of compliance-moat regulation. The regulation imposed a comprehensive regime of data rights, consent requirements, data minimization principles, and breach notification obligations on any entity processing personal data of EU residents — regardless of where the entity is located. Violations carry penalties of up to €20 million or 4% of global annual turnover, whichever is higher.

The mechanism creating a compliance moat operates as follows: GDPR imposes high fixed costs for compliance — legal assessment, technical infrastructure, process redesign, staff training, DPO appointment, and ongoing documentation. These fixed costs are largely scale-insensitive: a startup processing the personal data of 10,000 users faces many of the same structural compliance obligations as a large enterprise. This cost asymmetry creates a substantial barrier to entry for small new entrants, while benefiting incumbents that have already absorbed the fixed compliance investment. Simultaneously, the regulatory uncertainty itself creates demand for expert intermediaries who can operationalize compliance on behalf of organizations that lack internal expertise.

#### Literature Evidence

European data protection authorities levied €1.2 billion in fines during 2024 alone, a figure that demonstrates sustained regulatory pressure rather than one-time enforcement activity. The enforcement environment systematically advantages firms with mature compliance programs while penalizing late movers. The IAPP (International Association of Privacy Professionals) estimated in 2019 that Fortune 500 companies spent approximately $7.8 billion on GDPR compliance in the first two years, a figure that represents sunk cost creating lock-in to the compliance-operational model they built.

#### Implementations and Benchmarks

The clearest market creation story in the GDPR ecosystem is OneTrust. Founded in 2016 — two years before GDPR's enforcement date — in explicit anticipation of the regulation, OneTrust built a privacy management platform that tracks consent, manages data subject requests, and automates compliance documentation. By 2022, OneTrust had captured a 29.7% share of the global data privacy compliance software market, more than twice the share of its nearest competitor, and achieved a $5.3 billion valuation. The company grew 73% faster year-over-year than the rest of the market. TrustArc, an earlier entrant dating to 1997, raised $70 million in 2019 on the strength of GDPR tailwinds. The global GDPR software and tools market was valued at $2.83 billion in 2024 and is projected to reach $6.84 billion by 2030 (CAGR: 15.64%). A broader framing that includes GDPR-adjacent services projects the market at $3.34 billion in 2025 growing to $10.23 billion by 2030 at a 25.11% CAGR.

The compliance moat created by GDPR has also benefited large technology platforms in ways consistent with the Stigler capture model. A 2020 study found that GDPR enforcement disproportionately disadvantaged small ad-tech firms, which lacked the resources to achieve certification, while large platforms with existing legal infrastructure absorbed compliance costs and maintained their network advantages. Meta, Google, and Amazon — the companies with the most to lose from GDPR's data minimization requirements — have also proved most capable of meeting its requirements while smaller competitors exited the market.

#### Strengths and Limitations

The GDPR compliance moat model creates durable, long-duration advantages. Regulatory regimes once established are rarely fully repealed; they more often accumulate. The expansion of GDPR-model frameworks to the UK (UK GDPR), California (CCPA/CPRA), Brazil (LGPD), and India (DPDP Act 2023) means that the total addressable market for privacy compliance tools continues to expand as new jurisdictions adopt the template.

The key limitation is that compliance moat advantages are most defensible during the period of regulatory ambiguity. As enforcement patterns solidify and the compliance solution space matures, the market commoditizes: open-source compliance tools, off-the-shelf consulting packages, and regulatory guidance reduce the marginal value of proprietary compliance platforms. This suggests that the highest-margin period in a compliance moat market is the first 3-7 years post-regulation, before the solution set has fully standardized.

### 4.2 Mandated Data Portability: Open Banking and PSD2

#### Theory and Mechanism

The EU's Second Payment Services Directive (PSD2), fully effective from September 2019, mandated that banks expose customer account data and payment initiation capabilities to authorized third-party providers (TPPs) via standardized APIs. This is the canonical example of what we term mandated portability regulation: the state effectively nationalizes an incumbent's data infrastructure, requiring it to be made available to competitors as a public utility.

The market creation mechanism in mandated portability is structurally different from the compliance moat. Rather than creating opportunity through the *cost* of compliance, it creates opportunity through the *access* that compliance enables. PSD2 gave authorized fintechs access to data that previously required either bank partnerships (which incumbents could selectively grant and revoke) or screen-scraping (which was technically fragile and legally contested). With API access mandated, the distribution bottleneck previously controlled by banks was broken open.

The two primary categories of authorized third-party providers under PSD2 are Account Information Service Providers (AISPs), which can aggregate account data to provide consolidated views and analytics, and Payment Initiation Service Providers (PISPs), which can initiate payments directly from a user's bank account — bypassing card network fees.

#### Literature Evidence

The global open banking market was valued at approximately $31.61 billion in 2024 and is projected to reach $135.17 billion by 2030, growing at a CAGR of 27.6%. Europe maintained the largest regional share at 36.4% in 2024. Deloitte's global survey of open banking frameworks identifies two broad models: regulatory-driven models (EU, UK, Australia) where mandated API access creates the infrastructure, and market-driven models (India, Singapore, Japan) where policy incentives but not legal mandates promote data sharing. The regulatory-driven model has consistently produced faster market formation, with the UK's open banking ecosystem — launched via the Competition and Markets Authority's 2018 banking reform — generating over 7 million active users within five years.

#### Implementations and Benchmarks

The open banking wave created a generation of fintech companies whose entire product premise depended on regulatory access that did not exist pre-PSD2. Plaid, the account connectivity infrastructure company, was built on the technical substrate of open banking and screen-scraping, reaching a $13.4 billion valuation before antitrust concerns derailed its acquisition by Visa in 2021. Tink (acquired by Visa for $1.8 billion in 2022), Truelayer, Yapily, and Token.io were all built as pure-play open banking infrastructure layers. Revolut, Monzo, and N26 used open banking as a distribution mechanism to aggregate competing bank products within their own interfaces.

Australia extended the model further with its Consumer Data Right (CDR), which applies beyond financial services to energy and telecommunications, creating a cross-sector open data infrastructure. The OECD's 2022 survey documents this expansion: more than 20 countries have moved or are moving toward open finance frameworks that extend portability requirements to investment accounts, insurance, and pension data.

McKinsey's analysis of PSD2 identifies three waves of value creation: first, account aggregation and financial management (largely commoditized by 2022); second, credit underwriting using alternative data (growing); and third, embedded finance and business process automation (nascent). Each wave represents a successive deepening of the market opportunity created by the initial regulatory mandate.

#### Strengths and Limitations

Mandated portability creates markets rapidly — the open banking fintech wave was underway within 18 months of PSD2 enforcement. However, the competitive advantages created are less durable than compliance moats. Once the regulatory infrastructure is established, the marginal cost of accessing mandated APIs falls to near zero, and advantage shifts to product design, distribution, and network effects rather than regulatory access itself. The first-generation open banking players (account aggregators) have already experienced significant commoditization, with pricing for account connection APIs declining approximately 30-40% between 2020 and 2024.

A secondary limitation is regulatory reversal risk via enforcement drift: the mandated portability model requires continuous regulatory enforcement against incumbents who prefer closed data architectures. Where enforcement lapses — as has occurred in some EU member states where bank API quality remains poor — the market creation effect is muted.

### 4.3 Regulatory Unlocks: Telehealth, Cannabis, Psychedelics, and Crypto

#### Theory and Mechanism

Prohibition reversal and regulatory unlock operate through a distinct mechanism from the previous two categories. Where compliance moats create markets through friction and mandated portability creates markets through access, regulatory unlocks release markets that were being actively suppressed by legal prohibition or administrative restriction. The suppressed demand pre-exists the regulation; the regulation merely determines whether it can be commercially served.

This mechanism has a distinctive temporal signature: a time-compressed market formation burst in the immediate post-unlock period, followed by consolidation, as multiple competitors race to claim positions in a newly legal market before it normalizes. The window for first-mover advantage is typically shorter and more intense than in compliance moat or portability scenarios.

#### 4.3.1 Telehealth

Telehealth is a case of regulatory unlock driven not by political reform but by public health emergency. Prior to March 2020, Medicare reimbursement for telehealth services was restricted to rural originating sites, required in-person establishment visits before telehealth follow-ups, and excluded most service categories. The COVID-19 public health emergency declaration triggered automatic waivers of these restrictions, effectively deregulating telehealth overnight.

The market response was immediate. Telehealth utilization surged 154% during the acute pandemic period. The global telehealth market reached $123-186 billion by 2024-2025 (estimates vary by scope) and is projected to reach $1.27 trillion by 2034, growing at a CAGR of approximately 24.6%. North America accounts for approximately 45-48% of this market.

Companies that had built infrastructure in anticipation of the regulatory unlock captured disproportionate gains. Teladoc Health's revenue grew from $553 million in 2019 to $2.0 billion in 2022. Hims & Hers Health went public via SPAC in 2021 at a $1.6 billion valuation and reached approximately $1.5 billion in revenue by 2024 by combining telehealth infrastructure with direct-to-consumer pharmaceutical fulfillment. The model — using telehealth access as a distribution mechanism for prescription products — was only viable at scale after the regulatory unlock.

However, the telehealth unlock is characterized by a critical limitation: regulatory temporariness. As of early 2026, virtually all Medicare telehealth flexibilities remain in place on temporary extensions rather than permanent statutory reform. DEA controlled substance prescribing waivers have been extended four times. The threat of expiration creates sustained uncertainty for capital allocation in the sector and limits the durability of incumbency advantages built on regulatory access. This is a category where the timing window remains open but is not permanently secured.

#### 4.3.2 Cannabis

Cannabis legalization represents the most economically complete example of prohibition reversal to date. From Colorado and Washington's adult-use legalizations in 2012 through the 40-state medical and 24-state adult-use landscape in 2024, the industry grew from zero to $30.1 billion in legal U.S. annual sales, generating $4.4 billion in state tax revenue from adult-use sales alone and employing approximately 425,000 full-time workers.

The market creation mechanism here is pure prohibition reversal: demand was suppressed, not absent. A 2020 National Survey on Drug Use and Health estimated that approximately 49 million Americans used cannabis in 2019 — a market that existed entirely outside the legal economy. Legalization did not create demand; it captured demand and channeled it through regulatory frameworks that created distinct commercial opportunities: licensed cultivation, extraction, retail, compliance services, and cannabis-specific fintech (addressing the cash-only limitation imposed by federal banking restrictions).

The temporal dynamics of cannabis legalization illustrate the gold rush-and-consolidation pattern typical of prohibition reversals. Early state legalizations (Colorado, Washington) produced extraordinary margins for first licensed operators. As more states legalized, price compression and interstate competition intensified. California's market — the largest potential in the country — has been characterized by persistent dominance of the illicit market due to high tax burdens and licensing delays, demonstrating that prohibition reversal creates opportunity but does not guarantee market formation without supporting regulatory infrastructure.

Federal scheduling conflicts remain the primary structural risk. Cannabis companies are excluded from federal bankruptcy protections, face higher effective tax rates under IRS Section 280E, and cannot access federally insured banking — limitations that would be resolved by federal rescheduling or legalization and that represent the next major regulatory catalyst for the sector.

#### 4.3.3 Psychedelics

Psychedelic therapeutics represents a regulatory unlock in early stages. Oregon's Measure 109 (2020) created the world's first licensed psilocybin service framework, with licensed service centers opening in 2023. Colorado's Proposition 122 (2022) established a similar framework. As of 2024, 24 states have introduced legislation related to psychedelic research or decriminalization.

The global psychedelic therapeutics market was valued at $3.07 billion in 2024 and is projected to reach $8.33 billion by 2032 (CAGR: 13.3%). A more aggressive estimate projects growth from $2.94 billion in 2025 to $11.03 billion by 2034 (CAGR: 15.82%). The 2024 National Defense Authorization Act directed the DoD to fund clinical research into psychedelic treatments for PTSD and traumatic brain injury, signaling federal interest that could accelerate the unlock timeline.

The key regulatory variable is FDA approval: MDMA-assisted therapy for PTSD was recommended for approval by an FDA advisory panel but ultimately rejected in August 2024, a significant setback. Psilocybin has received "Breakthrough Therapy" designation from the FDA for multiple indications, which expedites review. The market creation pattern for psychedelics is conditional on this clinical-regulatory pathway, creating a binary risk structure unlike the more gradual cannabis unlock.

From a market structure perspective, the psychedelics sector faces the "pharma-versus-wellness" classification challenge: if psilocybin achieves FDA approval as a medicine, it will be distributed through clinical channels with pharmaceutical-level pricing; if it evolves primarily through state wellness licensing (the Oregon model), it will be distributed through service-center frameworks at consumer prices. These are structurally different markets requiring different competitive positioning.

#### 4.3.4 Crypto

Cryptocurrency regulation represents a hybrid of prohibition reversal and standards creation. The EU's Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA) became fully applicable on December 30, 2024, establishing the world's first comprehensive statutory framework for crypto-asset service providers. The U.S. enacted the GENIUS Act in July 2025, its first federal law specifically governing payment stablecoins. The SEC's approval of Bitcoin spot ETFs in January 2024 marked the entry of institutional-grade regulated investment products.

These regulatory developments created multiple distinct market opportunities. The clearest is institutional custody and investment: Bitcoin spot ETPs reached $175 billion in holdings within 18 months of approval, with institutions including Fidelity, BlackRock, and Citigroup expanding crypto product offerings on the strength of regulatory clarity. The second opportunity is compliance infrastructure: MiCA's requirements for reserve backing, audited monthly disclosures, AML compliance, and consumer protection standards created demand for compliance tooling that did not exist at scale before the regulatory framework. Chainalysis, Elliptic, TRM Labs, and similar blockchain analytics firms saw sustained demand growth as regulatory obligations expanded.

Tokenized real-world assets — tokenized money market funds reached $8 billion AUM and tokenized gold exceeded $3.5 billion AUM by December 2025 — represent a third category enabled by regulatory clarity around asset custody and disclosure.

The key insight from crypto regulation as a market creation mechanism is that regulatory clarity, even when it imposes constraints, functions as a trust-creation mechanism that expands the addressable market by enabling institutional participation that was previously impeded by compliance uncertainty.

### 4.4 Right-to-Repair and Consumer Empowerment Regulation

#### Theory and Mechanism

Right-to-repair regulation belongs to the consumer empowerment category of the taxonomy: it corrects information and access asymmetries between manufacturers and consumers by mandating that consumers and independent repairers have access to the tools, parts, and documentation needed to repair products. The market creation mechanism is the inverse of the compliance moat: rather than creating barriers to entry, right-to-repair creates market access for a previously suppressed repair and secondary-parts ecosystem.

#### Literature Evidence and Regulatory Timeline

The EU Right to Repair Directive (Directive 2024/1799) was adopted on June 13, 2024, with a national implementation deadline of July 31, 2026. The directive requires manufacturers to repair listed products at fair prices after warranties expire and mandates that spare parts and repair documentation be made available to independent repairers. A central EU repair platform is mandated by July 2027. The covered product categories include household appliances (washing machines, dryers, refrigerators), smartphones, and tablets, with additional categories (vacuum cleaners, printers, computers) under development.

In the United States, California enacted its Right to Repair Act, effective July 1, 2024, requiring manufacturers of electronics sold in California to provide repair tools, parts, and documentation for products costing $50 or more. As of 2024, at least 47 states had introduced right-to-repair legislation, though only a handful had enacted it.

#### Implementations and Market Opportunities

The market creation effect of right-to-repair operates along several distinct dimensions:

**Independent repair sector**: The formal market for independent electronics repair services benefits from guaranteed access to OEM parts and documentation that was previously either unavailable or prohibitively expensive. The global smartphone repair market was valued at approximately $4.2 billion in 2023 and is expected to grow to $6.1 billion by 2028, with the regulatory mandate likely to accelerate this expansion by normalizing the repair transaction.

**Secondary parts marketplace**: Authorized access to OEM components creates structured secondary parts markets. iFixit, which had operated in an informal relationship with manufacturers, gained formal standing as a repair documentation repository. Platforms specializing in certified used components benefit from the formalization of what had been a grey market.

**Design-for-repairability services**: The EU's repairability scoring requirement — manufacturers must provide a standardized repairability score for listed products — creates demand for product design consulting services focused on disassembly optimization, component accessibility, and repair documentation systems. This is an example of regulation creating not just a product market but a professional services category.

**Circular economy infrastructure**: The directive's goal of extending product lifespans creates structural demand for refurbishment logistics, inspection, and certification infrastructure. Companies positioned in the refurbishment supply chain — reverse logistics operators, certified refurbishers, warranty underwriters for repaired products — benefit from mandatory extension of the product lifecycle.

#### Strengths and Limitations

Right-to-repair regulation creates markets with relatively long duration and low reversal risk: the circular economy rationale has strong political durability in the EU context, and the economic case for repair over disposal strengthens as material and energy costs increase. However, the market creation effect is contingent on enforcement quality. Apple's compliance with California's right-to-repair law has been criticized as technical compliance that maintains practical friction through pricing of parts and complexity of repair processes. The gap between regulatory text and operational compliance determines how much market actually opens.

The most significant structural limitation is that right-to-repair regulation creates market opportunity primarily for small and medium-sized repair enterprises rather than venture-scale businesses. The market expansion is real but diffuse, occurring across millions of individual repair transactions rather than in concentrated platform markets. This affects the type of capital that can efficiently participate in the opportunity.

### 4.5 AI Regulation and the Compliance Product Category

#### Theory and Mechanism

The EU AI Act, which entered into force on August 1, 2024, with full applicability targeted by August 2, 2026, represents the most consequential AI governance framework to date. It establishes a risk-tiered classification system for AI applications — prohibited, high-risk, limited risk, minimal risk — with extensive documentation, conformity assessment, and transparency requirements for high-risk systems. Simultaneously, the NIST AI Risk Management Framework (RMF), ISO/IEC 42001, and emerging state-level AI regulations in the United States have created a complex multi-jurisdictional compliance environment.

The market creation mechanism parallels the GDPR compliance moat model: a new regulatory framework creates compliance obligations that most organizations lack the internal capability to meet, generating demand for specialized tools, consulting services, and auditing infrastructure. The AI Act additionally creates a standards creation effect: by mandating specific technical documentation formats, model cards, and risk assessment procedures, it creates an ecosystem of interoperable compliance artifacts that enable third-party auditing and certification industries.

#### Literature Evidence and Market Data

The EU AI Act compliance market is projected to range between €7.6 billion and €38 billion in total value by 2030, with a central estimate of approximately €3.4 billion annually for compliance software, consulting, and certification by 2030, assuming approximately 18% of AI systems are classified as high-risk. The broader AI compliance monitoring market (global) was valued at $1.8 billion in 2024 and is projected to reach $5.2 billion by 2030, growing at a CAGR of 19.4%.

As of 2024, 13.5% of EU enterprises — approximately 364,000 companies — use at least one AI technology, with adoption highest (over 40%) among large enterprises. The compliance demand is concentrated among large enterprise AI deployers and AI system providers to high-risk sectors (healthcare, employment, critical infrastructure, law enforcement).

A critical market timing dynamic: the EU AI Act's compliance pressure concentrates in mid-2026, when the high-risk provisions take full effect. Market analysts anticipate a compliance rush in Q2-Q3 2026 as companies race to complete required documentation and audits, with a corresponding spike in demand for AI auditing, certification, and documentation tooling.

#### Implementations and Benchmark Companies

Several categories of companies are building specifically around AI Act compliance:

- **AI auditing platforms**: Companies providing technical auditing of AI systems against risk classification criteria (Credo AI, Arthur AI, Fiddler AI).
- **Documentation and governance tooling**: Platforms automating AI system cards, bias testing, and audit trail generation (AI Verify, IBM OpenScale, Microsoft Responsible AI tools).
- **Legal and regulatory advisory**: Law firms and consultancies with EU AI Act specializations, analogous to the GDPR advisory industry.
- **Regulatory intelligence platforms**: Compliance.ai and similar platforms that track AI regulatory changes across jurisdictions.
- **Certification bodies**: Technical conformity assessment bodies authorized under the AI Act to certify high-risk AI systems — a role that requires formal accreditation and creates a limited-entry professional services market.

The AI Act also created a regulatory sandbox provision — Article 57 mandates that member states establish AI sandboxes to allow testing of innovative AI systems under reduced regulatory constraints. This sandbox mechanism mirrors the FCA fintech sandbox model and is expected to attract AI startups seeking regulatory certainty while building compliance track records.

#### Strengths and Limitations

The AI compliance market has several distinctive characteristics that distinguish it from the GDPR model. First, the compliance obligations are technically more complex: AI system documentation requires not just legal procedures but technical expertise in model evaluation, explainability, and bias assessment. This creates a more durable moat for technically sophisticated compliance providers compared to the procedural compliance tools of the privacy market.

Second, the geopolitical dimension adds complexity. The EU AI Act applies extraterritorially to AI systems deployed in the EU regardless of origin, but the U.S. has moved toward deregulatory posture under the Trump administration, which rescinded Biden's executive order on AI safety in January 2025. This creates a regulatory divergence where EU compliance requirements do not map to U.S. requirements, potentially fragmenting the market but also creating cross-border compliance management demand.

The primary limitation is regulatory uncertainty about scope and enforcement. The AI Act's risk classification system requires individual determinations about whether specific AI applications qualify as high-risk, a determination that remains contested across many use cases. Until authoritative enforcement decisions establish precedent, compliance tool providers face the challenge of building products against ambiguous regulatory specifications.

### 4.6 ESG Mandates, Carbon Markets, and Sustainability Regulation

#### Theory and Mechanism

ESG and sustainability regulation operates through a diffuse mandate cascade mechanism: top-level regulatory requirements on large organizations propagate down through supply chains and investment portfolios, creating secondary demand for compliance products, measurement services, and sustainable product substitutes at every tier. The EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), California's climate disclosure requirements, and mandatory carbon pricing schemes in the EU, UK, Canada, and China represent the regulatory infrastructure generating this cascade.

The mechanism creates markets at multiple levels simultaneously:
- **Reporting and measurement tools**: ESG data collection, verification, and reporting platforms for organizations subject to mandatory disclosure.
- **Carbon markets**: Compliance carbon allowances (EU ETS, California cap-and-trade, RGGI) and voluntary carbon credits traded as instruments for meeting net-zero commitments.
- **Sustainable products and supply chain substitution**: Consumer and business demand shifts toward products with better ESG credentials as purchasing decisions are influenced by mandatory disclosures.
- **Greenwashing enforcement infrastructure**: As enforcement of ESG claims intensifies, legal, auditing, and certification services become necessary.

#### Literature Evidence and Market Data

The global voluntary carbon credit market was valued at approximately $2.4-4.0 billion in 2024 (estimates vary significantly by methodology) and is projected to reach $23.99 billion by 2030 at a CAGR of 35.1%. Compliance carbon markets are larger and less volatile: the EU ETS is the world's largest carbon market, with annual trading volumes in the hundreds of billions of euros. Over 60% of S&P 500 companies reported using carbon credit markets in 2023.

The ISSB (International Sustainability Standards Board) standards have been adopted or are being implemented in approximately 30 jurisdictions representing over 40% of global market capitalization. The EU's CSRD, the most stringent mandatory ESG reporting framework in force, requires first reports in 2025 and applies to approximately 50,000 European companies. Interoperability guidance between ISSB standards, EU standards, and other frameworks is enabling a "global reporting passport" approach, reducing fragmentation costs.

The ESG legal and compliance environment intensified dramatically in 2024. Australia's securities regulator imposed a record AUS$12.9 million penalty for misleading ESG claims. The UK CMA received authority to fine up to 10% of global turnover for environmental noncompliance. The EU adopted rules banning generic environmental claims ("eco-friendly," "sustainable") without substantiation, effective 2026.

Harvard Law School's end-of-year review documents the significant jurisdictional divergence: the EU and California continue to expand ESG obligations while the U.S. federal government under the Trump administration has moved toward rollback of SEC climate disclosure requirements. This divergence creates a market for cross-jurisdictional ESG compliance management — organizations operating globally must satisfy EU requirements regardless of U.S. federal posture.

#### Implementations and Market Structure

The ESG market creation effect manifests most clearly in several product categories:

**ESG data and reporting platforms**: Workiva, Sustainalytics, MSCI ESG, Refinitiv ESG, and Bloomberg ESG data have all grown substantially as mandatory disclosure obligations increased. The ESG data and analytics market is estimated to exceed $1.3 billion annually and is growing rapidly.

**Carbon accounting and management tools**: Companies like Watershed, Persefoni, Plan A, and Sweep have built carbon accounting platforms specifically around mandatory and voluntary corporate disclosure requirements. Watershed reached unicorn status ($1 billion valuation) in 2022 on the strength of corporate net-zero commitment demand.

**Green bonds and sustainable finance**: The green bond market reached $575 billion in issuance in 2023, with sustainability-linked bonds adding another $150 billion. These instruments exist specifically as regulatory-compliant financial products that allow issuers to access capital at preferential rates from ESG-constrained investors.

**Consumer sustainability certification**: Certification labels (B Corp, LEED, carbon-neutral certification) function as compliance proxies in consumer markets, creating demand for certification infrastructure and third-party auditing.

The key tension in the ESG market is what Harvard's review identifies as the "greenwashing enforcement paradox": aggressive enforcement of ESG claims is raising the bar for what constitutes credible sustainability marketing, which simultaneously raises demand for credible ESG products and services while creating legal risk for organizations that overclaim. This dynamic is expanding the total addressable market for ESG compliance infrastructure while winnowing the competitive field.

### 5.1 Cross-Cutting Observations

**Observation 1: The compliance moat pattern produces the most durable market advantages.** GDPR and AI Act compliance markets share a structural feature: the compliance obligation is technically complex and firm-specific, generating high switching costs once an organization has embedded a compliance platform into its operations. This is distinct from portability markets where access is commoditized, or unlock markets where the first-mover advantage is primarily temporal.

**Observation 2: Reversal risk is primarily a function of whether the regulation reflects durable political consensus or emergency authority.** Telehealth flexibilities, based on public health emergency authority, carry the highest reversal risk of any domain analyzed. Cannabis federal prohibition persists despite widespread state-level reform, creating ongoing structural risk. GDPR, the EU AI Act, and the EU Right to Repair directive reflect multi-year legislative processes with strong cross-party support, creating more durable regulatory foundations.

**Observation 3: The timing window in mandated portability markets is shorter than commonly perceived.** The first wave of open banking products (account aggregation) commoditized within approximately 3-4 years of PSD2 enforcement. Late entrants to the PSD2 ecosystem building account aggregation products in 2022-2023 faced a mature, price-compressed market. This suggests that in portability markets, the competitive advantage accrues primarily to the infrastructure layer (Plaid, Tink) rather than product layer, and that subsequent application-layer opportunities require building on top of commoditized access.

**Observation 4: Geographic sequencing matters in prohibition reversal markets.** Cannabis markets in early-legalizing states (Colorado, Washington, Oregon) have structurally different economics than late-legalizing states (New York, New Jersey), because early markets built brand and operational infrastructure before competition intensified. The same pattern is expected to hold in psychedelics (Oregon's early mover advantage is already being established) and would apply to any future federal-level unlocks.

**Observation 5: ESG mandates create the most diffuse market opportunity.** Unlike compliance moats (concentrated in software vendors) or prohibition reversals (concentrated in licensed operators), ESG mandates propagate demand across dozens of product and service categories simultaneously. This diffusion creates less concentrated competitive dynamics but also more total economic activity.

## 6. Open Problems and Gaps

**6.1 Regulatory Reversal Economics**

The analysis of reversal risk in this paper is largely qualitative. A productive research direction would be developing quantitative models of regulatory reversal probability as a function of observable political variables (legislative majority margins, industry lobbying expenditure, public opinion polling, judicial challenge frequency), enabling more systematic assessment of the durability of regulatory market creation.

**6.2 The Regulatory Complexity-Innovation Tradeoff**

The existing literature conflates "regulation" as a uniform treatment. The distinction between protective and restrictive regulation, while useful, is still coarse. A more granular taxonomy distinguishing between process requirements (compliance moats), access mandates (portability), prohibition/permission (unlocks), reporting obligations (ESG), and standards settings would enable cleaner empirical identification of which specific regulatory instruments generate market creation versus market constraint.

**6.3 The Second-Order Effects of Compliance Moats**

Most analysis of GDPR-style compliance moats focuses on their direct market creation effect (the privacy software market). Less work has examined second-order effects: the extent to which compliance moats entrench large incumbent platforms in adjacent markets, and whether this entrenchment creates net positive or negative welfare outcomes. The observation that GDPR disproportionately disadvantaged small ad-tech firms while benefiting large platforms warrants more systematic empirical study.

**6.4 Global Regulatory Arbitrage in AI**

The divergence between EU AI Act requirements and U.S. deregulatory posture creates a natural experiment in regulatory arbitrage for AI deployment. Companies that develop AI systems under EU compliance frameworks may have different product characteristics (more documentation, explainability features, bias testing) than systems developed without those requirements. Tracking how this divergence affects market outcomes — whether compliance-grade AI products develop a quality premium in global markets — would generate important evidence for regulatory design.

**6.5 The Gig Economy Benefits Gap as an Unsolved Market Creation Problem**

The gig economy regulatory domain is the most underdeveloped in terms of clear market creation. Approximately 80 million Americans were gig workers in 2024, over 63% of whom reported feeling financially vulnerable due to lack of portable benefits. The Brookings Institution's December 2024 roundtable on portable benefits and EU Platform Workers Directive (2024) represent early-stage regulatory development that, if translated into U.S. law, would create a market for portable benefits infrastructure estimated in the tens of billions of dollars annually. Stride Health and Catch have built early products in this space, but the regulatory catalyst for full market creation has not yet arrived. This is a watched-but-waiting opportunity.

**6.6 Measurement of the Full Market Creation Effect**

Current market sizing for regulation-created markets focuses on the compliance/tooling layer. A comprehensive measurement would include: (a) the direct market created for products/services that only exist because of the regulation, (b) the market expanded by the regulation through demand activation, (c) the market destroyed or displaced by the regulation, and (d) the transfer payments between market participants. No comprehensive accounting of this type exists for any major regulatory market creation event.

---

## 7. Conclusion

The central argument of this paper is that regulation creates markets as reliably as it constrains them, and that the mechanism of creation determines the structural characteristics of the resulting market. Compliance moats produce concentrated, durable competitive advantages for early sophisticated entrants; mandated portability produces rapid market formation that quickly commoditizes; prohibition reversal produces time-compressed gold rush dynamics with high winner-take-most characteristics; and ESG mandate cascades produce diffuse, long-duration demand creation across many product categories simultaneously.

The empirical evidence reviewed is substantial. GDPR created a $6+ billion global privacy compliance market and minted a $5.3 billion company (OneTrust) that had not existed before the regulation. PSD2 bootstrapped a $31.6 billion open banking infrastructure market. Cannabis legalization unlocked $30.1 billion in legal annual U.S. sales from what had been a purely shadow economy. The EU AI Act compliance market is projected at $3.4 billion annually before the regulation is even fully in force. The telehealth regulatory unlock created a $123+ billion global market from a set of emergency pandemic measures that, five years on, have not been reversed.

These are not coincidences. They are instances of a generalizable pattern: government regulatory activity is a primary driver of market structure in advanced economies, and the direction of this effect is not uniformly constraining. The investor, entrepreneur, and product strategist who treats regulation as purely a friction to manage is leaving unrealized the observation that the most durable markets of the past decade have been built not in opposition to regulatory change but in anticipation of it.

The theoretical synthesis offered here — integrating Stigler's capture model, public interest correction theory, and more recent work on protective versus restrictive regulation — suggests that the deepest explanation for this pattern is structural. Regulation resolves coordination problems, reduces uncertainty for investment, establishes standard interfaces that enable ecosystem development, and releases suppressed demand through permission-granting. These are all market-creating mechanisms, and they operate independently of whether any specific regulation was well-designed or efficiently enforced.

The open problems identified in Section 6 — particularly around quantifying reversal risk, tracking second-order incumbency effects of compliance moats, and measuring the full economic incidence of regulatory market creation — represent the frontier for rigorous research in this area. As the regulatory landscape continues to evolve, with AI governance, climate disclosure, and digital market regulation all in active development across major jurisdictions simultaneously, the need for systematic frameworks to assess regulatory market creation has arguably never been greater.
