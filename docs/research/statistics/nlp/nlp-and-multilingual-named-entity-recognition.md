# NLP and Multilingual Named Entity Recognition for Swiss‑Specific PII with Transformer Models

*28 February 2026*  

## Abstract  

This survey examines multilingual named entity recognition (NER) with a focus on transformer‑based models, personally identifiable information (PII) detection, and Swiss‑specific constraints on person and location entities. It documents how general‑purpose multilingual encoders, legal and clinical de‑identification systems, and hybrid rule–neural architectures address recall gaps for PERSON and LOCATION categories, exemplified by practical performance levels around 0.68 for PERSON and 0.52 for LOCATION in out‑of‑domain Swiss text. The analysis concentrates on cross‑lingual transfer, domain adaptation, and privacy‑preserving requirements that jointly shape design choices rather than prescribing any particular solution.  

The survey identifies five main families of approaches: off‑the‑shelf multilingual transformers fine‑tuned on generic NER corpora, domain‑ and region‑adapted transformers for legal and administrative texts, hybrid NER–PII pipelines combining neural models with gazetteers and regular expressions, cross‑lingual and few‑shot transfer methods for low‑resource or dialectal settings, and privacy‑specific de‑identification frameworks built around transformer encoders. These families differ systematically in data requirements, robustness to code‑switching and orthographic variation, and their alignment with Swiss and European data protection law. Evidence from recent benchmarks such as WikiANN, MultiCoNER, UNER, WiNNL, MultiLeg, and Swiss legal corpora reveals heterogeneous PERSON and LOCATION performance across languages and domains, with legal and clinical contexts often demonstrating stronger recall once in‑domain adaptation and PII‑focused annotation are available. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2208.14536.pdf)

Key trade‑offs emerge around generality versus jurisdictional specificity, parameter efficiency versus cross‑lingual coverage, and privacy protection versus downstream utility. Large multilingual encoders like XLM‑R and mBERT demonstrate strong zero‑shot or few‑shot transfer on PERSON and LOCATION categories in many languages, including European ones relevant for Switzerland, but exhibit sensitivity to domain shift, dialectal variation, and fine‑grained address patterns. In‑domain pretraining and hybrid PII architectures raise recall on legally salient entity types (for example, court parties, case numbers, and structured identifiers) yet may narrow linguistic coverage or require large labeled corpora that are costly to assemble under strict anonymisation rules. [aclweb](https://www.aclweb.org/anthology/2020.acl-main.747.pdf)

## 1. Introduction  

The problem addressed in this survey concerns how multilingual NER systems, particularly transformer‑based architectures, detect PERSON and LOCATION entities as PII in a multilingual, legally constrained setting such as Switzerland. This question matters because Swiss public communication, court decisions, and administrative documents regularly mix German, French, Italian, English, and dialectal variants, while regulatory frameworks like the Swiss Data Protection Act (DPA), the Human Research Act (HRA), and the EU GDPR impose stringent requirements on de‑identification of names, addresses, and other identifiers. In practical deployments, practitioners frequently observe moderate recall for PERSON and especially LOCATION categories on Swiss text outside canonical benchmarks, which motivates a systematic examination of available modeling strategies. [forscenter](https://forscenter.ch/wp-content/uploads/2022/02/kleinerstam_fg11_anonymisation1_v1.1_final_2022.pdf)

The scope of this survey covers NER approaches where multilingual transformers are central to the architecture, including mBERT, XLM‑R, and related encoder or encoder–decoder models applied to sequence labeling or span classification. It considers both generic NER datasets (for example, WikiANN and UNER) and domain‑specific corpora in legal and clinical domains, with particular attention to systems explicitly designed for de‑identification or pseudonymisation tasks. Work that focuses solely on monolingual English NER without multilingual transfer relevance, on pure rule‑based de‑identification without any neural component, or on image or speech NER beyond text transcriptions is excluded. Swiss‑specific resources enter the analysis when they inform domain adaptation (for example, Swiss Federal Supreme Court rulings) or highlight challenges posed by Swiss German dialects and regionally specific corpora. [noe-eva.github](https://noe-eva.github.io/NOAH-Corpus/)

The survey uses several key definitions. In plain language, identifying the names of people, places, and organizations in text is called named entity recognition; formally, NER is a sequence labeling task assigning entity class tags such as PER, LOC, or ORG to token spans. Multilingual NER refers to a setting where models operate across many languages using shared parameters, often leveraging joint pretraining on large multilingual corpora; transformer‑based multilingual encoders such as XLM‑R (Cross‑lingual Language Model RoBERTa) and multilingual BERT (mBERT) instantiate this concept. PII denotes any information that can identify an individual either directly, such as a full name or precise address, or indirectly, such as combinations of demographics; in Swiss and EU legal frameworks, PII includes names, addresses, dates of birth, identifiers, and sometimes case numbers or unique document IDs. Throughout, PERSON and LOCATION categories are treated as core PII‑relevant classes, although many systems extend beyond these. [bjmc.lu](https://www.bjmc.lu.lv/fileadmin/user_upload/lu_portal/projekti/bjmc/Contents/10_3_18_Viksna.pdf)

## 2. Foundations  

The theoretical foundation for multilingual NER lies in distributed representation learning combined with sequence modeling. Transformer encoders implement self‑attention mechanisms that map token sequences to contextual embeddings capturing long‑range dependencies, which are then decoded into BIO or BIOES tag sequences indicating entity boundaries. In multilingual transformers like XLM‑R, a single shared subword vocabulary and masked language modeling objective across approximately one hundred languages produce language‑agnostic representations that facilitate cross‑lingual transfer, especially for structurally similar languages and shared scripts. This mechanism explains why such models often achieve strong zero‑shot NER performance in languages lacking task‑specific annotation when fine‑tuned on resource‑rich languages, though interference between unrelated languages and tokenization issues can degrade results in more distant language pairs. [ieeexplore.ieee](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/11181790/)

Classical NER systems typically relied on conditional random fields (CRFs) with hand‑crafted lexical, orthographic, and gazetteer features, while later neural approaches introduced bidirectional LSTMs combined with CRF decoders. Transformer‑based models subsume many of these design elements by learning context‑sensitive features from large corpora, with CRF or span‑classification heads often acting as structured decoders on top of transformer embeddings. Empirical studies across languages like Latvian, Hindi, Marathi, and various European tongues show that fine‑tuned XLM‑R frequently outperforms mBERT and BiLSTM‑CRF baselines on NER benchmarks, although carefully trained monolingual transformers can exceed multilingual ones when substantial in‑language data is available. This evidence positions multilingual transformers as robust baselines for cross‑lingual NER, especially where creating monolingual models for each language would be impractical. [ijece.iaescore](https://ijece.iaescore.com/index.php/IJECE/article/view/35275)

For PII detection and de‑identification, NER serves as one component within larger privacy‑preserving pipelines. Clinical and legal de‑identification systems apply transformers, often domain‑adapted variants like ClinicalBERT or custom RoBERTa models, to identify names, addresses, IDs, and other PHI categories, then integrate post‑processing modules that mask, redact, or pseudonymise the detected spans. Evaluations on benchmark datasets such as i2b2 2014 demonstrate that transformer‑based de‑identification models can achieve high recall (often above 0.95) on names and dates, while exhibiting more variable performance on less standardized identifiers like phone numbers or internal hospital codes, which depend strongly on format regularities. In the Swiss and broader European legal context, de‑identification frameworks must also align with statutory definitions of anonymised and pseudonymised data under the DPA, HRA, and GDPR, which emphasize irreversibility of re‑identification at reasonable effort and elevate addresses, dates of birth, and unique numbers as elements requiring masking. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12719064/)

The Swiss linguistic landscape introduces specific challenges for multilingual NER and PII detection. Swiss German dialects lack standardized orthography, feature high lexical variability, and commonly appear in social media, chat, and informal text, complicating tokenization and lexicon coverage for models trained primarily on standard German. Corpora such as NOAH’s Corpus of Swiss German Dialects and the emerging Swiss German podcast datasets provide resources for part‑of‑speech tagging and speech processing but rarely include NER or PII annotations, so current de‑identification systems mostly rely on transfer from High German, English, or other resource‑rich languages. Beyond dialectal variation, Swiss‑specific PII includes addresses formatted with “CH‑” postal codes, canton abbreviations, and multi‑lingual street and locality names, which are only partially covered in generic multilingual NER datasets dominated by Wikipedia and news text. This constellation of factors explains why off‑the‑shelf multilingual NER often under‑detects PERSON and LOCATION entities in Swiss data despite strong performance on international benchmarks. [github](https://github.com/noe-eva/NOAH-Corpus)

## 4. Analysis  

### 4.1 Off‑the‑shelf multilingual transformers (Approach A)  

**Theory & mechanism.**  

Off‑the‑shelf multilingual transformers operate by pretraining a single encoder on large multilingual corpora with masked language modeling and possibly translation objectives, then fine‑tuning that encoder on NER data using a token‑classification head or CRF decoder. XLM‑R, for example, is trained on approximately two terabytes of filtered CommonCrawl text in one hundred languages, creating shared subword representations that support cross‑lingual contextualization of person and location names even in languages with limited supervision. During NER fine‑tuning, the model learns label distributions for tags like B‑PER or B‑LOC, which it then applies to new texts, relying on transfer of orthographic and contextual patterns across languages that share scripts or entity naming conventions. This mechanism yields high accuracy for benchmark languages but may under‑represent Swiss German dialects, region‑specific toponyms, and legal or administrative naming styles if those are under‑sampled in the pretraining data. [arxiv](http://arxiv.org/pdf/2209.12616.pdf)

**Literature evidence.**  

The XLM‑R paper demonstrates that multilingual pretraining at scale improves NER performance over mBERT, reporting an average gain of approximately 2.40 F1 points across cross‑lingual NER benchmarks. A study of multilingual transformers for NER in Baltic and European languages finds that fine‑tuned XLM‑R models achieve the best results on multilingual datasets, outperforming mBERT and BiLSTM‑CRF baselines on F1 scores for PER, ORG, and LOC, though monolingual LitLat BERT remains superior on purely Latvian data. Work on WiNNL v1, a multilingual Wikinews NER benchmark, reports that XLM‑R and related models obtain strong span‑level F1 scores across eleven European languages, including French and German, while revealing performance variation linked to dataset domain and language‑specific annotation artifacts. Additional evaluations on Hindi and Marathi NER show that multilingual XLM‑R performs best for Hindi, whereas monolingual MahaRoBERTa dominates Marathi, highlighting conditions where multilinguality is advantageous. [link.springer](https://link.springer.com/10.1007/978-981-19-6088-8_56)

**Implementations & benchmarks.**  

The T‑NER library offers standardized fine‑tuning pipelines for transformer‑based NER and is widely used to evaluate XLM‑R, mBERT, and other encoders across cross‑domain and cross‑lingual settings, including WikiANN and CoNLL datasets. WikiANN itself provides NER annotations for PER, LOC, and ORG in 176 languages derived from Wikipedia, enabling broad evaluation of PERSON and LOCATION recall, which is especially relevant for multilingual deployments in Swiss contexts involving German, French, Italian, and English. Studies benchmarking few‑shot multilingual NER across models like XLM‑R, mBERT, CANINE, and mT5 report that XLM‑R and mT5 generally deliver superior macro‑F1, particularly in under‑represented languages, although performance still depends heavily on the amount and domain match of the few‑shot data. Application‑oriented work on forest‑fire tweet monitoring in Indonesian documents that XLM‑R achieves precision, recall, and F1 around 0.92 for LOCATION and TIME extraction, illustrating high in‑domain performance on a single language but leaving cross‑jurisdiction generalization open. [ijeecs.iaescore](https://ijeecs.iaescore.com/index.php/IJEECS/article/view/40707)

**Strengths & limitations.**  

Off‑the‑shelf multilingual transformers demonstrate strong baseline performance on PERSON and LOCATION detection where the target language and domain resemble pretraining and fine‑tuning data, as evidenced by high F1 scores on WikiANN, WiNNL, and various national NER datasets. These models scale well across many languages because a single encoder can support NER in multiple tongues, making them attractive when Swiss deployments must cover German, French, Italian, Romansh, and English simultaneously. However, evidence from dialectal corpora such as Swiss German and from specialized legal datasets suggests that domain shift, orthographic variation, and domain‑specific entity categories systematically erode recall, particularly for nuanced locations and regionally distinctive person names. This family therefore appears most effective as a baseline or “first pass” NER solution, with limitations arising in Swiss‑specific domains where PERSON and LOCATION detection gaps similar to those motivating this survey are commonly reported. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2310.04632.pdf)

### 4.2 Domain‑ and region‑adapted transformers (Approach B)  

**Theory & mechanism.**  

Domain‑ and region‑adapted transformers extend the off‑the‑shelf paradigm by further pretraining or fine‑tuning the encoder on large unlabelled or weakly labeled corpora from a specific domain or jurisdiction, then training an NER or PII classifier on annotated data from the same context. The mechanism relies on adapting the model’s internal representations to capture domain‑specific collocations, citation styles, and naming conventions, such as how Swiss court rulings refer to parties, locations, and case identifiers, or how Indonesian legal decisions mention judges and prosecutors. This process effectively shifts the prior distribution over token contexts so that PII categories like PERSON and LOCATION appear in patterns that the model has seen frequently during adaptation, which tends to raise recall for those categories given enough in‑domain examples. [aclanthology](https://aclanthology.org/2023.nllp-1.16.pdf)

**Literature evidence.**  

A study on Indonesian legal entity recognition (IndoLER) constructs a dataset of court decisions with twenty fine‑grained legal entity types and compares multilingual and monolingual transformers, concluding that RoBERTa‑based models (IndoRoBERTa and XLM‑R) outperform deep BiLSTM and BiLSTM‑CRF baselines on extracting legal roles and organizations. Work on German legal NER describes a dataset of approximately sixty‑seven thousand sentences from federal court decisions and reports that BERT‑based models fine‑tuned on this corpus achieve substantial gains over CRF baselines for PER, LOC, and legal‑specific entity classes, illustrating the benefit of domain‑specific supervision for legal texts. Most directly relevant for Swiss contexts, research on automatic anonymisation of Swiss Federal Supreme Court rulings enhances an existing rule‑based system by training BERT‑based models, including variants pre‑trained on in‑domain legal texts, to recognize entities requiring anonymisation, such as person names, organizations, geographic locations, and case numbers; the in‑domain pretraining yields additional improvements beyond generic BERT. [aclanthology](https://aclanthology.org/2020.lrec-1.551.pdf)

**Implementations & benchmarks.**  

The German legal NER dataset and its associated models provide a concrete implementation where BERT‑based encoders are fine‑tuned for legal entity extraction, supporting evaluations across entity types that overlap with PII categories like person and location. The Swiss Federal Supreme Court anonymisation work describes a practical anonymisation pipeline deployed in production, where NER models assist in identifying parties and sensitive details that must be masked before publication; experimental results show that pretraining on legal decisions in German improves precision and recall relative to general‑purpose BERT models. Similar strategies appear in domain‑specific corpora such as E‑NER, an English legal NER corpus, where transformer models trained specifically on legal text outperform generic models for person and location recognition within legal judgments. These implementations collectively document that domain‑adapted transformers can align NER with legal citation structures and recurring entity patterns not present in general news or Wikipedia corpora. [aclanthology](https://aclanthology.org/2022.nllp-1.22.pdf)

**Strengths & limitations.**  

Domain‑ and region‑adapted transformers demonstrate particular strength in legal and administrative contexts, where evidence indicates that in‑domain pretraining raises recall for entities like parties, locations, and identifiers that exhibit idiosyncratic formatting. For Switzerland, such models align naturally with the strong anonymisation requirements imposed on court decisions and administrative acts, because training data explicitly encodes which spans must be anonymised under Swiss practice. However, these approaches depend on large volumes of domain text, often subject to legal and privacy constraints, making corpus construction and annotation expensive and legally sensitive, particularly when PII cannot easily be shared across institutions. In addition, specialization on legal or administrative style may reduce robustness on other Swiss text genres such as social media in Swiss German or multilingual journalistic discourse, where PERSON and LOCATION detection may again fall below levels observed in curated legal corpora. [sphn](https://sphn.ch/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/Homburger-memorandum_Swiss-Legal-Framework-for-De-identification-of-Health-Related-Data_20210105.pdf)

### 4.3 Hybrid NER–PII pipelines (Approach C)  

**Theory & mechanism.**  

Hybrid NER–PII pipelines integrate neural NER models with rule‑based components such as regular expressions, gazetteers, and user‑maintained lists to capture PII patterns that are highly structured, jurisdiction‑specific, or poorly represented in training data. In this mechanism, a transformer‑based NER model provides context‑aware tagging for relatively unstructured entities like person and organization names, while deterministic modules identify addresses, ID numbers, and specific lexical items based on format or membership in curated lists. Post‑processing then merges overlapping spans, resolves conflicts, and applies redaction or pseudonymisation policies; for example, canton abbreviations or Swiss municipality names can be recognized via gazetteers, whereas arbitrary person names rely more heavily on the neural model’s contextual understanding. [ceur-ws](https://ceur-ws.org/Vol-2624/abstract1.pdf)

**Literature evidence.**  

The Anonymise tool, designed for multilingual document pseudonymisation, exemplifies this hybrid strategy by combining deep‑learning NER based on multilingual transformers, a regular expression module implemented with Presidio, and list‑based matching; it supports entity types including person, ID number, location, organization, URL, date, amount, nationality, and profession. Evaluation on the MultiLeg dataset, a multilingual legal corpus with fine‑grained PII labels, shows that Anonymise achieves recall above 0.93 and an F1 score around 0.85, with relatively uniform performance across English, Estonian, Latvian, and Lithuanian, whereas alternative tools show stronger language dependence. Earlier work on an anonymisation toolkit presented at SwissText describes a system for all EU languages that uses BERT‑based NER, regular expressions, and preprocessing to detect personal data such as names, addresses, emails, and bank accounts, underscoring the feasibility of combining neural models with rule‑based processing in European legal contexts. Complementary frameworks like Philter further show that integrating blacklists, whitelists, and part‑of‑speech features with statistical models enhances precision by preventing over‑redaction of ambiguous terms. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2410.01648v1)

**Implementations & benchmarks.**  

Anonymise is implemented as a modular system with three main components: a transformer‑based NER model trained on the MultiLeg dataset, a Presidio‑based regex engine extended with language‑specific recognizers for national ID numbers, and an editable list module for sensitive strings; it supports multiple pseudonymisation strategies and exposes both a web interface and an API. The MultiLeg dataset itself contains parallel multilingual legal texts from the Court of Justice of the European Union, annotated with PII‑relevant entity types such as person, location, organization, ID number, and amount, providing a benchmark where PERSON and LOCATION detection can be evaluated in a setting structurally similar to many European legal corpora. Swiss‑focused discussions in data anonymisation guides emphasize that addresses, dates of birth, and unique identification numbers must be irreversibly masked to achieve anonymisation under Swiss law, implying a natural role for regex and list‑based components in capturing standardized Swiss postal codes, canton abbreviations, and health identifiers that neural NER alone may miss. [aclanthology](https://aclanthology.org/2025.ranlp-1.154/)

**Strengths & limitations.**  

Hybrid NER–PII pipelines exhibit strength where PII categories have regular formats or well‑defined vocabularies, as demonstrated by the high recall Anonymise attains across several languages and entity types in the MultiLeg benchmark. For Swiss applications, this approach aligns with the need to detect structured identifiers like Swiss social security numbers, “CH‑” postal codes, and canton abbreviations, which can be robustly captured through regex and gazetteers even when transformer NER struggles with sparse evidence. At the same time, hybrid systems add design and maintenance overhead, particularly for multilingual environments where lists and patterns must be curated for each language and legal culture, and there is evidence that rule‑based components can reduce precision if not carefully tuned, especially when over‑matching common tokens in free text. These systems also inherit any biases and coverage gaps of the underlying multilingual transformers for PERSON and LOCATION names, so they often reduce but do not fully eliminate recall shortfalls analogous to the 0.68 PERSON and 0.52 LOCATION levels motivating this survey. [acl-bg](https://acl-bg.org/proceedings/2025/RANLP%202025/pdf/2025.ranlp-1.154.pdf)

### 4.4 Cross‑lingual and few‑shot transfer (Approach D)  

**Theory & mechanism.**  

Cross‑lingual and few‑shot transfer approaches leverage multilingual encoders to project NER competence from high‑resource source languages to low‑resource or dialectal targets, including languages relevant for Swiss contexts like Romansh or Swiss German. One mechanism aligns representations across parallel corpora by training an entity alignment model on top of XLM‑R that projects entities detected on the English side to the target language side, thereby generating pseudo‑labeled data for zero‑shot NER. Another mechanism uses self‑training or pseudo‑labeling, where a multilingual transformer fine‑tuned on available languages labels large unlabeled corpora in additional languages; the highest confidence predictions are then used as training data, as in systems built for the MultiCoNER shared tasks on complex NER. Few‑shot approaches fine‑tune models with very small annotated samples in the target language, relying on the pretrained encoder to generalize from shared subword and contextual patterns learned across many languages. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.13515)

**Literature evidence.**  

The XLM‑R alignment work for cross‑lingual NER demonstrates that using parallel corpora to project entity labels from English into the target language yields better zero‑shot NER accuracy than previous unsupervised methods, illustrating the value of alignment modeling for PER and LOC in languages lacking annotation. The MultiCoNER dataset and associated SemEval shared tasks provide large‑scale multilingual benchmarks explicitly focused on complex entities and noisy inputs, including code‑mixed queries and short search snippets; participating systems using XLM‑R with span classification losses and self‑training achieve competitive rankings across multilingual and code‑mixed tracks, indicating that multilingual transformers can handle substantial cross‑lingual complexity with appropriate training regimes. The OpenNER 1.0 initiative aggregates and standardizes more than thirty NER datasets across fifty‑one languages, enabling systematic evaluation of multilingual and cross‑lingual NER performance, while WiNNL v1 and UNER introduce multilingual Wikinews and Wikipedia‑based NER resources that cover predominantly European languages with complex entity hierarchies. Studies on low‑resource languages such as Hiligaynon and Roman Urdu further show that mBERT and XLM‑R can reach F1 scores above 0.80 on PER and LOC with limited annotation, especially when combined with architectures like IDCNN–BiLSTM–CRF. [ieeexplore.ieee](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/11229319/)

**Implementations & benchmarks.**  

MultiCoNER and its successor MultiCoNER II constitute core benchmarks for cross‑lingual, complex NER, providing datasets over eleven or more languages with difficult entity types and code‑mixed content; XLM‑R‑based systems with span classification and pseudo‑labeling consistently rank among the strongest participants. UNER and WiNNL v1 add further multilingual NER corpora, including hierarchical entity labels and Wikinews articles in languages like French and German, enabling evaluations more directly relevant to Swiss media contexts. WikiANN supplies broad coverage across 176 languages, useful for gauging zero‑shot PERSON and LOCATION performance in numerous tongues, although its Wikipedia basis may not reflect legal or administrative text styles. Collectively, these resources allow practitioners to quantify how multilingual transformers generalize from high‑resource European languages to low‑resource varieties and dialects, even when only few‑shot annotation is available. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2305.03300.pdf)

**Strengths & limitations.**  

Cross‑lingual and few‑shot transfer methods display notable strengths in extending NER coverage to languages and varieties with minimal labeled data, especially when those share scripts and lexical material with training languages, as in many European settings. For Swiss use cases, these methods are conceptually attractive for handling Romansh and possibly certain Swiss German writing styles, where collecting large NER corpora would be onerous. Nevertheless, the supporting literature reports mixed results for structurally distant languages and dialects with non‑standard orthography, suggesting that Swiss German’s highly variable spellings could pose substantial challenges absent additional adaptation. Furthermore, current cross‑lingual benchmarks seldom capture jurisdiction‑specific PII formats or legal anonymisation practices, so improvements in generic PER and LOC F1 may not translate directly into compliance‑relevant recall levels in Swiss legal or administrative contexts. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.10776)

### 4.5 Privacy‑specific de‑identification frameworks (Approach E)  

**Theory & mechanism.**  

Privacy‑specific de‑identification frameworks build end‑to‑end systems around transformer models, optimizing for high recall of PII categories defined by legal or institutional standards, often in clinical or legal text. Their mechanism typically includes a dedicated NER or sequence‑labeling model trained on PHI annotations (names, addresses, dates, IDs), followed by a redaction or pseudonymisation stage that removes or replaces detected spans, and possibly a quality assurance step that checks for residual PII. Some frameworks integrate large language models (LLMs) as de‑identifiers, using prompt engineering and in‑context learning to instruct models like GPT‑4 to identify and redact PII, without fine‑tuning; others rely on domain‑fine‑tuned BERT or RoBERTa models and treat LLMs as auxiliary components. This architecture is driven by regulatory constraints such as HIPAA in the United States and the GDPR and national data protection laws in Europe, which specify categories of PHI and define conditions under which data is considered anonymised or pseudonymised. [physionet](https://www.physionet.org/content/transformer-deid/1.0.0/tests/)

**Literature evidence.**  

The Transformer‑DeID project provides BERT, DistilBERT, and RoBERTa‑based models for de‑identifying free‑text clinical notes, trained and evaluated on the i2b2 2014 dataset; these models use token‑level NER to detect PHI and report strong performance when evaluated on binary PHI versus non‑PHI labeling. A recent benchmarking study of transformer‑based models and LLMs for medical record de‑identification compares systems including Microsoft Azure’s de‑identification service, RoBERTa and BERT models, AnonCAT, GPT‑4, Llama‑3, and Phi‑3, finding that the best models identify approximately ninety‑five percent of all PII and achieve recall above 0.97 for names, with more variable performance on medical record numbers and phone numbers. The study also documents that performance can vary substantially across datasets, even within the clinical domain, highlighting the sensitivity of de‑identification systems to data source and annotation schemes. Additional work proposes multi‑layered de‑identification frameworks that combine ClinicalBERT NER with rule‑based tools like Philter and LLM‑based components such as DeID‑GPT, underscoring the trend toward hybrid, transformer‑centric architectures to meet stringent privacy requirements. Furthermore, work comparing de‑identified versus synthetic clinical notes suggests that removing PHI tokens alone may not prevent membership inference attacks, indicating that de‑identification must be considered within a broader privacy–utility trade‑off. [nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-81170-y)

**Implementations & benchmarks.**  

Transformer‑DeID releases NER models and code via PhysioNet, enabling replication and adaptation of BERT‑based de‑identification pipelines on clinical corpora. The aforementioned benchmarking study evaluates multiple models on several clinical datasets, providing recall and precision metrics per PII category and per dataset, which supply quantitative evidence about where transformer‑based PHI detection performs reliably and where residual risk remains. In the European and Swiss context, legal analyses of de‑identification frameworks detail statutory criteria for anonymised versus pseudonymised data and emphasize that names, addresses, dates of birth, and unique identifiers must be irreversibly masked to meet anonymisation thresholds under Swiss law, tying technical de‑identification mechanisms to legal definitions. These implementations and studies together show that privacy‑specific frameworks constitute a distinct family of NER‑based systems optimized for regulatory compliance and risk reduction rather than general‑purpose entity extraction. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12719064/)

**Strengths & limitations.**  

Privacy‑specific de‑identification frameworks demonstrate high recall on PHI categories in well‑studied domains like English clinical notes, with several transformer and LLM‑based systems attaining recall near or above 0.98 for names and dates, which surpasses typical off‑the‑shelf NER performance on comparable tasks. Their tight integration with regulatory frameworks and explicit modeling of PHI categories align naturally with Swiss and European anonymisation requirements, particularly when jurisdiction‑specific attributes like case numbers, national identifiers, or health data usage constraints are encoded in the annotation scheme. However, these frameworks often rely on domain‑specific English corpora and may not directly transfer to multilingual, legally complex settings like Switzerland without substantial new annotation and adaptation; moreover, studies on membership inference attacks reveal that even thoroughly de‑identified notes may leak information, suggesting that NER‑based de‑identification is necessary but not sufficient for robust privacy. Within Swiss contexts, the coexistence of multiple legal regimes (DPA, HRA, GDPR) and multilingual communication further complicates the design and evaluation of such systems beyond what current benchmarks capture. [physionet](https://www.physionet.org/content/transformer-deid/1.0.0/tests/)

## 6. Open Problems & Gaps  

- **Swiss‑specific multilingual PII corpora.** There is a notable lack of publicly available, richly annotated NER and PII datasets that cover core Swiss languages (German, French, Italian, Romansh), Swiss German dialects, and mixed‑language administrative or media text, with explicit labeling of PERSON, LOCATION, and structured PII categories aligned to Swiss legal requirements. Addressing this gap would require coordinated corpus collection under Swiss data protection law, standardized anonymisation protocols during annotation, and sustained funding for legal and linguistic expertise. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.19866)

- **Dialectal and code‑switched Swiss German NER.** Existing Swiss German resources such as NOAH’s Corpus mainly target part‑of‑speech tagging and dialect identification, leaving NER and PII tasks under‑explored despite the prevalence of dialectal content in Swiss digital communication. Future work would need to explore character‑level or byte‑level multilingual models, noise‑robust training, and cross‑dialectal transfer to capture PERSON and LOCATION entities in highly variable orthographies while maintaining acceptable precision. [noe-eva.github](https://noe-eva.github.io/NOAH-Corpus/)

- **Evaluation protocols for legal de‑identification under Swiss law.** While Swiss and European legal analyses articulate conceptual requirements for anonymisation and pseudonymisation, there is limited empirical work defining standardized evaluation suites for de‑identification systems on Swiss legal texts comparable to i2b2 in the clinical domain. Developing such benchmarks would require consensus on entity taxonomies, sampling of diverse court and administrative documents, and metrics that reflect both residual re‑identification risk and the utility of anonymised texts for downstream analysis. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2310.04632.pdf)

- **Privacy–utility trade‑offs beyond PHI token removal.** Recent evidence that de‑identified clinical notes remain vulnerable to membership inference attacks even after PHI removal indicates that NER‑based de‑identification addresses only part of the privacy problem, while preserving many statistical regularities exploited by attackers. Extending this line of work to Swiss legal and administrative data would require modeling adversaries’ knowledge, considering distributional properties beyond explicit PHI, and designing evaluation frameworks that jointly measure de‑identification effectiveness and task utility in realistic settings. [nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-81170-y)

- **Cross‑lingual PERSON and LOCATION robustness metrics.** Benchmarks such as WikiANN, UNER, and MultiCoNER provide multi‑language NER evaluation but do not explicitly target robustness of PERSON and LOCATION detection under domain shift, adversarial perturbations, or jurisdiction‑specific naming conventions. Constructing robustness‑oriented test suites, potentially including Swiss‑specific names and place names as stress cases, could clarify how far current multilingual transformers are from closing observed recall gaps such as 0.68 for PERSON and 0.52 for LOCATION in real‑world Swiss deployments. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2212.07429.pdf)

## 7. Conclusion  

This survey has documented a landscape of multilingual NER approaches centered on transformer models, with particular emphasis on PERSON and LOCATION detection as PII in Swiss‑relevant contexts. Off‑the‑shelf multilingual encoders like XLM‑R and mBERT demonstrate strong performance on generic benchmarks such as WikiANN, UNER, WiNNL, and MultiCoNER, where F1 scores for PER and LOC often exceed 0.85 in high‑resource languages, yet evidence from dialectal and legal corpora indicates notable performance degradation under domain and orthographic shift. Domain‑adapted transformers trained on legal or administrative decisions, including work on Indonesian legal documents, German court rulings, and Swiss Federal Supreme Court anonymisation, appear to recover much of this gap by aligning model representations with jurisdiction‑specific entity structures and citation practices, albeit at the cost of substantial annotation and governance overhead. [aclanthology](https://aclanthology.org/2024.dlnld-1.3.pdf)

Hybrid NER–PII pipelines, cross‑lingual transfer strategies, and privacy‑specific de‑identification frameworks provide complementary axes of variation in this design space. Hybrid systems like Anonymise and EU anonymisation toolkits exploit regular expressions, gazetteers, and list matching to improve detection of structured identifiers and legal PII categories across multiple European languages, including many that share characteristics with Swiss corpora. Cross‑lingual and few‑shot methods using XLM‑R and related models extend NER coverage to low‑resource and dialectal languages, although Swiss German’s orthographic variability and the absence of annotated NER corpora present unresolved challenges, while privacy‑specific frameworks in clinical domains show that transformer‑based de‑identification can achieve high PHI recall yet still leave open broader questions about re‑identification risk and legal adequacy. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.10776)

Across these approaches, structural trade‑offs arise between generality and specialization, label scarcity and model capacity, as well as legal compliance and data utility. Multilingual transformers trained on broad corpora appear to offer a solid foundation for multilingual PER and LOC detection, but their performance in Swiss contexts depends heavily on domain adaptation, integration with hybrid PII components, and the availability of evaluation benchmarks reflecting Swiss law and linguistic diversity. The gaps identified in §6, particularly around Swiss‑specific corpora, dialectal NER, and standardized de‑identification evaluation, delineate areas where further research appears crucial for understanding how close current systems are to closing practitioner‑observed PERSON and LOCATION detection gaps in Swiss multilingual environments.
